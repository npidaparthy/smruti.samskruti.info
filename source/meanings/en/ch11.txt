# 11.1
short: Arjuna says — By this supreme secret teaching about the Self that You have spoken out of compassion for me — this delusion of mine has been dispelled.
long: Chapter 11 opens with Arjuna's gratitude — the teaching of chapters 7-9 has cleared his delusion. "Paramam guhyam adhyātma-saṃjñitam" — the supreme secret called Adhyātma. "Yat tvayoktam vacas tena moho'yaṃ vigato mama" — by Your word, this delusion has departed. The stage is set for the most dramatic event in the Gita: Arjuna, his mind somewhat cleared, will now ask to see what no human has ever seen.
wbw:
  mad-anugrahāya | phrase | out of compassion for me
  paramaṃ guhyam | adjective phrase | the supreme secret
  adhyātma-saṃjñitam | adjective | called/designated Adhyātma
  yat tvayoktaṃ vacas tena | phrase | by which word spoken by You
  moho'yaṃ vigato mama | phrase | this delusion of mine has departed
---
# 11.2
short: O Lotus-eyed One, I have heard from You at length the origin and dissolution of beings, and Your inexhaustible greatness.
long: Arjuna summarizes what he has learned: "śrutam" — heard — the chapters on creation, dissolution, and divine greatness. "Bhavāpyayau hi bhūtānāṃ" — the arising and passing away of beings. "Māhātmyam api cāvyayam" — and Your inexhaustible (avyayam) greatness. This is the preparation: Arjuna has received the conceptual teaching. But concepts have limitations. He is now about to ask for direct vision — not hearing about the divine but actually seeing it.
wbw:
  bhavāpyayau hi bhūtānāṃ | phrase | the arising and dissolution of beings
  śrutau vistaraśo mayā | phrase | heard at length by me
  tvattaḥ kamala-patrākṣa | phrase | from You, O lotus-eyed one
  māhātmyam api cāvyayam | phrase | and also Your inexhaustible greatness
---
# 11.3
short: O Supreme Lord, just as You have described Yourself, I wish to see Your divine form, O Supreme Person.
long: "Evam etad yathāttha tvam ātmānaṃ parameśvara / draṣṭum icchāmi te rūpam aiśvaraṃ puruṣottama." Just as You have described Yourself (evam etad yathāttha tvam) — all the claims of chapters 7-10 — Arjuna now wants to see them directly. "Draṣṭum icchāmi te rūpam aiśvaram" — I wish to see Your sovereign/divine form. The shift from śruti (hearing) to darśana (direct vision) is the pivot of the chapter. Arjuna is asking for the supreme grace of direct divine vision.

  evam etad yathāttha tvam | phrase | just as You have described Yourself
  ātmānaṃ parameśvara | phrase | Your own self, O Supreme Lord
  draṣṭum icchāmi te rūpam | phrase | I wish to see Your form
  aiśvaraṃ puruṣottama | adjective phrase | divine/sovereign, O Supreme Person
---
# 11.4
short: O Lord, if You think it possible for me to see this — O Yoga-Lord, then show me Your immutable Self.
long: "Manyase yadi tac chakyaṃ mayā draṣṭum iti prabho / yogeśvara tato me tvaṃ darśayātmānam avyayam." If You think it possible (yadi manyase tat śakyam) — Arjuna is appropriately humble. He does not demand; he requests. "Yogeśvara" — O Lord of Yoga — acknowledging that the power to grant this vision belongs entirely to Krishna. "Darśayātmānam avyayam" — show me Your immutable Self. The humility of the request contrasts beautifully with the enormity of what will be granted.

  yogeśvara tato me tvaṃ | phrase | O Lord of Yoga, then You to me
  darśayātmānam avyayam | imperative phrase | show Your immutable Self

# 11.5
short: The Lord says — O Pārtha, behold My hundreds and thousands of divine forms of many kinds, of various colors and shapes.
long: "Paśya me pārtha rūpāṇi śataśo'tha sahasraśaḥ / nānāvidhāni divyāni nānāvarṇākṛtīni ca." Behold (paśya) My forms — hundreds, thousands. "Nānāvidhāni divyāni" — many kinds, divine. "Nānāvarṇākṛtīni" — of many colors and shapes. The plurality — hundreds, thousands — signals that what Arjuna is about to see cannot be reduced to any single image or form. The divine is not one shape but a living multiplicity that defies finite description. The viśvarūpa darśana begins.

# 11.6
short: Behold the Ādityas, Vasus, Rudras, the Aśvins, the Maruts — behold many wonders not seen before, O Bhārata.
long: A cosmic roll-call: Ādityas (12 solar deities), Vasus (8 elemental gods), Rudras (11 storm deities), the two Aśvins (divine healers), and Maruts (the wind gods, 49 in number). "Paśyādityān vasūn rudrān aśvinau marutas tathā" — the entire Vedic pantheon within one vision. "Bahūny adṛṣṭa-pūrvāṇi paśyāścaryāṇi bhārata" — see many wonders never seen before. The vision encompasses not just the deities that Arjuna knows from tradition but forms beyond any prior conception.

  bahūny adṛṣṭa-pūrvāṇi | phrase | many never-seen-before
  paśyāścaryāṇi bhārata | phrase | behold wonders, O Bhārata

# 11.7
short: O Gudākeśa, see here today the entire universe — moving and unmoving — gathered in My body, and whatever else you wish to see.
long: "Ihaika-sthaṃ jagat kṛtsnaṃ paśyādya sa-carācaram / mama dehe guḍākeśa yac cānyad draṣṭum icchasi." Here (iha), in one place (eka-stham), see the entire universe (jagat kṛtsnam) — moving and unmoving (sa-carācaram) — gathered in My body. "And whatever else you wish to see" — a magnificent generosity: whatever Arjuna has ever wondered about is included in this vision. The cosmos compressed into one form. Time, space, all beings, all events — simultaneously visible in the divine body.

  mama dehe guḍākeśa | locative phrase | in My body, O Gudākeśa
  yac cānyad draṣṭum icchasi | phrase | and whatever else you wish to see

# 11.8
short: But you cannot see Me with this eye of yours. I grant you divine sight — behold My supreme yoga-power.
long: "Na tu māṃ śakyase draṣṭum anenaiva sva-cakṣuṣā / divyaṃ dadāmi te cakṣuḥ paśya me yogam aiśvaram." You cannot (na śakyase) see Me with your own (sva) eye. The ordinary eye can only see the ordinary. Krishna grants divine sight (divyaṃ cakṣuḥ) — the capacity to perceive what transcends ordinary perception. This is the grace: the vision is not earned by Arjuna but granted. And the instruction: "paśya me yogam aiśvaram" — behold My divine yoga-power. The gift precedes the vision.
wbw:

  anena eva sva-cakṣuṣā | phrase | with this, your own eye
  divyaṃ dadāmi te cakṣuḥ | phrase | I grant you divine sight
  paśya me yogam aiśvaram | imperative phrase | behold My divine yoga-power

# 11.9
short: Sañjaya says — Having thus spoken, O King, Hari, the great Lord of yoga, then showed Arjuna His supreme, divine form.
long: Sañjaya narrates what Dhṛtarāṣṭra cannot see: "evaṃ uktvā tato rājan mahā-yogeśvaro hariḥ / darśayāmāsa pārthāya paramaṃ rūpam aiśvaram." Sañjaya, granted divine vision by Vyāsa (1.25), can describe what happened on Kurukṣetra. "Mahā-yogeśvaraḥ" — the great Lord of yoga — the grandest title. "Hariḥ" — Hari (remover of suffering, or golden-yellow). "Paramaṃ rūpam aiśvaram" — the supreme divine form. The description that follows is the poetic climax of the Bhagavad Gita.
wbw:
  evam uktvā tato rājan | phrase | having spoken thus, O King
  mahā-yogeśvaro hariḥ | noun phrase | the great Lord of yoga, Hari
  darśayāmāsa pārthāya | phrase | showed to Pārtha
  paramaṃ rūpam aiśvaram | phrase | the supreme divine form

# 11.10
short: (He showed) the form with many mouths and eyes, many wondrous aspects, many divine ornaments, many divine weapons upraised.
long: (11.11 continues) The description begins. "Aneka-vaktra-nayanam anekādbhuta-darśanam / aneka-divyābharaṇaṃ divyānekodyatāyudham." Many mouths and eyes — not a normal face but a multiplicity that represents all-seeing, all-speaking. Many wondrous aspects — each "aspect" (darśana) is itself a wonder. Many divine ornaments — adorned with cosmic beauty. Many divine weapons upraised — the active principle, ready to create and destroy. This is a form that does not fit into any human frame of reference.

# 11.11
short: (He showed the form) wearing divine garlands and garments, anointed with divine fragrances — all-wonderful, boundless, facing in all directions.
long: "Divya-mālyāmbara-dharaṃ divya-gandhānulepanam / sarvāścaryamayaṃ devam anantaṃ viśvato-mukham." Wearing divine garlands (mālyāmbara) — adorned with the beauty of all creation. Anointed with divine fragrances — all senses are engaged in this vision. "Sarvāścaryamayam" — all-wonder-composed — wonder itself is the substance of this form. "Anantam" — without end, infinite. "Viśvato-mukham" — facing in all directions simultaneously. No part of existence is outside this form's field of awareness. It faces everywhere at once.

# 11.12
short: If the radiance of a thousand suns were to blaze forth simultaneously in the sky — that might resemble the splendor of that Great Being.
long: "Divi sūrya-sahasrasya bhaved yugapad utthitā / yadi bhāḥ sadṛśī sā syād bhāsas tasya mahātmanaḥ." A thousand suns blazing simultaneously — even that might (syāt) approach the light of that Great Being. "Might" — even this image understates. The image is functionally impossible — a thousand suns would incinerate existence — and even so it only approximates. The scale of the viśvarūpa is beyond cosmic measure. Light that makes all other light seem dim.

# 11.13
short: There, in the body of the God of gods, the Pāṇḍava then saw the entire universe resting in one place — with its manifold divisions.
long: "Tatraika-sthaṃ jagat kṛtsnaṃ pravibhaktam anekadhā / apaśyad deva-devasya śarīre pāṇḍavas tadā." There, in one place (eka-stham), the entire universe (jagat kṛtsnam) — divided into many (pravibhaktam anekadhā). Arjuna sees it. Not the promise (7.7, 11.7) but the experience. All divisions — nations, species, times, events, cosmos — simultaneously present in one divine body. The many within the one, the one expressing as the many. The vision confirms everything Krishna has been saying.
wbw:

  pravibhaktam anekadhā | phrase | divided into many (divisions)

# 11.14
short: Then Arjuna, filled with wonder, with hair standing on end, bowing his head to the God with folded hands, spoke thus.
long: "Tataḥ sa vismayāviṣṭo hṛṣṭa-romā dhanañjayaḥ / praṇamya śirasā devaṃ kṛtāñjalir abhāṣata." The physical response to theophany: vismayāviṣṭa (filled with wonder/awe), hṛṣṭa-romā (hair standing on end — the classic śokaharṣa response of the body to overwhelming experience), praṇamya śirasā (bowing the head), kṛtāñjali (with folded hands). These are not Arjuna's usual warrior composure — this is the body responding to what the mind cannot contain. The response of a human being to the infinite.

# 11.15
short: Arjuna said — O God, I see all the gods in Your body — and all the various hosts of beings; and Brahmā, the Lord, seated on the lotus-throne; and all the sages and divine serpents.
long: Arjuna's speech begins with what he sees: "paśyāmi devāṃs tava deva dehe sarvāṃs tathā bhūta-viśeṣa-saṃghān / brahmāṇam īśam kamalāsana-stham ṛṣīṃś ca sarvān uragāṃś ca divyān." He sees all the devas, all beings, Brahmā seated on his lotus throne ("kamalāsana-stham"), all the sages, all the divine serpents. The vision is a living compendium of all mythology, all cosmology, all beings — simultaneously present in the divine body. Arjuna is overwhelmed by what he cannot categorize.

# 11.16
short: I see You with many arms, stomachs, mouths, and eyes — of infinite form — everywhere. I see no end, no middle, and no beginning of You, O Lord of All, Universal Form.
long: "Aneka-bāhūdara-vaktra-netraṃ paśyāmi tvāṃ sarvato'nanta-rūpam / nāntaṃ na madhyaṃ na punas tavādiṃ paśyāmi viśveśvara viśva-rūpa." Aneka (many) arms, stomachs, mouths, eyes — not a defined number but radical multiplicity. Sarvato'nanta-rūpam — of infinite form everywhere. "Nāntam na madhyam na punastava ādim" — no end, no middle, no beginning. The divine form is not located in space — it has no boundary points. It doesn't start or stop; it simply is. Viśveśvara (Lord of All), Viśvarūpa (Universal Form) — the grandest titles.

# 11.17
short: I see You with crown, mace, and discus — a mass of brilliance shining on all sides — difficult to gaze upon — blazing like a flaming fire and sun — immeasurable.
long: "Kirīṭinaṃ gadinaṃ cakriṇaṃ ca tejorāśiṃ sarvato dīptimantam / paśyāmi tvāṃ durnirīkṣyaṃ samantād dīptānalārka-dyutim aprameyam." The specific iconographic forms: kirīṭa (crown), gadā (mace), cakra (discus) — Viṣṇu's emblems. But the abstract dominates: "tejorāśim" — mass of brilliance. "Durnirīkṣyam" — difficult to look upon directly (like staring at the sun). "Dīptānalārka-dyutiḥ" — blazing like fire and sun. "Aprameyam" — immeasurable, beyond measure. The form overwhelms sight; the divine is both an object of vision and what makes vision impossible.

# 11.18
short: You are the Imperishable, the supreme to be known. You are the supreme refuge of this universe. You are the undying guardian of eternal dharma. You are the primeval Person — I believe this.
long: "Tvam akṣaraṃ paramaṃ veditavyaṃ tvam asya viśvasya paraṃ nidhānam / tvam avyayaḥ śāśvata-dharma-goptā sanātanas tvaṃ puruṣo mato me." Four declarations: akṣaram (the Imperishable), paramaṃ veditavyam (the supreme thing to be known), asya viśvasya paraṃ nidhānam (supreme treasure-house of this universe), avyayaḥ śāśvata-dharma-goptā (the undying guardian of eternal dharma), sanātana puruṣa (the primeval/eternal Person). "Mato me" — I believe this, I affirm this. Arjuna, in the midst of an overwhelming vision, is simultaneously formulating the highest theological affirmations.

# 11.19
short: Without beginning, middle, or end — of infinite power — with countless arms — with sun and moon as eyes — blazing fire as Your mouth — scorching this universe with Your radiance.
long: "Anādi-madhyāntam ananta-vīryam ananta-bāhuṃ śaśisūrya-netram / paśyāmi tvāṃ dīpta-hutāśa-vaktraṃ sva-tejasā viśvam idaṃ tapantam." Without beginning, middle, or end (anādi-madhyāntam) — truly infinite. Infinite power (ananta-vīryam), innumerable arms, sun and moon as eyes — the cosmic scale of the eyes sees by the light of the cosmic luminaries. "Dīpta-hutāśa-vaktra" — mouth blazing like a fire, consuming oblations. The entire universe scorched (tapantam) by the divine's own radiance. The vision is not comfortable — it is awesome in the original sense: awe-full.

# 11.20

The vision is simultaneously wonderful and terrible — "sublime" in the deepest sense.

# 11.23
short: Seeing Your great form with many mouths and eyes, O Mighty-armed One — with many arms, thighs, feet, bellies, and many terrible tusks — the worlds are terrified, and so am I.
long: "Rūpaṃ mahatte bahu-vaktra-netraṃ mahā-bāho bahu-bāhūru-pādam / bahūdaraṃ bahu-daṃṣṭrā-karālaṃ dṛṣṭvā lokāḥ pravyathitās tathāham." Arjuna now speaks from his own terror as well as observing the trembling of the worlds. "Bahu-daṃṣṭrā-kārālam" — terrible with many tusks. The tusks are a sign of the destructive/consuming aspect of the divine — like Kālī's teeth. "Lokāḥ pravyathitās tathāham" — the worlds tremble, and so do I. Arjuna is explicitly identifying with the cosmic trembling. He is not outside the vision; he is in it.
