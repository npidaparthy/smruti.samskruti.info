# 7.1
short: The Lord says — with mind fixed on Me, practicing yoga, taking refuge in Me, O Pārtha, hear how you shall know Me completely without doubt.
long: Chapter 7 opens with an intimate promise: practice yoga with mind on Me, take shelter in Me — and you shall know Me fully, without remainder. Krishna here shifts from technique (ch.6) to relationship. "Mayi āsakta-manāḥ" — mind attached to Me — is the seed. Everything else flows from that one-pointed orientation. Full knowledge (samagram mām) is the goal; complete refuge (mad-āśraya) is the method.

# 7.2
short: I shall teach you jñāna along with vijñāna in full — after knowing this, nothing more remains to be known in this world.
long: Jñāna here is theoretical/scriptural knowledge; vijñāna is direct experiential realization. Krishna promises both — not just intellectual understanding but lived knowing. "After knowing this, nothing more remains" — a bold claim that this teaching is complete, not partial. The two together constitute full knowledge of Brahman. One without the other is incomplete: knowledge without experience is dry, experience without knowledge is ungrounded.

# 7.3
short: Among thousands of humans, one strives for perfection; of those striving, perhaps one knows Me in truth.
long: The rarity of genuine seekers — and rarer still, those who actually attain. "Manuṣyāṇāṃ sahasreṣu" — among thousands. Not all seek liberation; of those who seek, not all realize. This isn't discouragement but calibration: what Krishna is about to reveal is the rarest knowledge precisely because so few pursue it sincerely to its end. The verse creates urgency: Arjuna, in this rare conversation, is receiving what most never access.

# 7.4
short: Earth, water, fire, air, ether, mind, intellect, and ego — these eight constitute My lower nature (aparā prakṛti).
long: The famous enumeration of the eight-fold nature. Five gross elements (pañca-mahābhūtas): earth, water, fire, air, ether. Then three subtle elements: mind (manas), intellect (buddhi), ego (ahaṃkāra). These are Krishna's aparā (lower/inferior) prakṛti — the material principle. This is everything perceivable and conceivable in the universe. Yet "lower" — there is something higher.

# 7.5
short: This is My lower nature; but beyond this, know My higher nature — the life-principle by which this universe is sustained.
long: The distinction is radical: the eight-fold material nature (7.4) is aparā — lower, inert, dependent. There is also a parā (higher, superior) prakṛti — the jīva, the living principle, consciousness itself. "Jīva-bhūtāṃ mahā-bāho yayedaṃ dhāryate jagat" — by which this entire universe is upheld. Consciousness is not a product of matter; matter is sustained within consciousness. This reverses ordinary materialist assumption entirely.

# 7.6
short: Know that all beings have their source in these two natures. I am the origin of this entire universe and also its dissolution.
long: The two natures (lower material + higher conscious) together account for all of existence. Every being is a combination. Krishna then makes the supreme claim: "I am the origin and dissolution of the entire universe." Not merely a cause among causes — the ultimate source. "Prabhavaḥ pralayaḥ" — arising and passing away. Both creativity and destruction are in Krishna. This establishes His absolute sovereignty over existence.

# 7.7
short: There is nothing higher than Me, O Dhanañjaya. All this is strung on Me as pearls on a thread.
long: The beautiful thread-and-pearls image. A thread runs through every pearl yet is invisible, holding all together — the pearls don't create the thread; the thread sustains the pearls. Krishna is the thread; all creation the pearls. "Mattaḥ parataraṃ nānyat" — nothing whatsoever is beyond Me. This is absolute non-dual claim: every diversity is held in one reality. Yet the thread doesn't merge with pearls — transcendence within immanence.

# 7.8
short: I am the taste in water, the radiance in the sun and moon, the sacred syllable OM in all the Vedas, the sound in ether, and the manhood in men.
long: The series of divine immanence begins — Krishna identifying Himself with the essential quality of each element. Not "I made water" but "I am the taste in water" — the very essence that makes water what it is. Rasa in water (the cool, life-giving quality), prabhā in sun/moon (the light itself), praṇava (OM) in Vedas, śabda in ākāśa (the carrier of all sound), pauruṣa in humans (the essential vitality). The divine is not distant; it is the innermost quality of everything.

# 7.9
short: I am the pure fragrance in earth, the brilliance in fire, the life in all beings, and the austerity in ascetics.
long: Continuing the litany: gandha (fragrance) in earth — the earthy smell that identifies the element. Tejas in fire — the heat-light that is fire's essence. Jīvana in all beings — the animating life-force itself. Tapas in tapasvis — the transformative heat of austerity. Each points to the same realization: wherever you find the essential quality of anything, you are touching the divine. Krishna is not hiding in remote temples; He is the quiddity of every thing.

# 7.10
short: Know Me as the eternal seed of all beings, O Pārtha. I am the intelligence of the intelligent, the splendor of the splendid.
long: "Bījaṃ māṃ sarva-bhūtānāṃ" — the seed of all beings. A seed contains the full tree in potential; Krishna is that primordial potentiality in all existence. Intelligence (buddhi) in the intelligent — not just any cleverness but that capacity for discrimination and wisdom that marks the truly intelligent. Tejas (splendor, vigor, radiance) in the vigorous. These aren't external qualities added to things — Krishna is what makes them genuinely themselves.

# 7.11
short: I am the strength of the strong, free from desire and passion. I am desire that is not contrary to dharma, O lord of the Bharatas.
long: Strength (bala) in the strong — specifically strength free from desire and passion (kāma-rāga-varjitam). Pure strength, not the aggrandizing kind. Then the subtle shift: "dharmāviruddho bhūteṣu kāmo'smi" — among beings, I am desire that does not contradict dharma. Krishna doesn't deny kāma (desire, love) — He purifies it. Desire aligned with dharma is divine. This prevents world-denial: natural, wholesome desire is a manifestation of the divine, not an obstacle to it.

# 7.12
short: Whatever states of being exist — sattvic, rajasic, or tamasic — know they are all from Me. Yet I am not in them; they are in Me.
long: The three guṇas — sattva (clarity/goodness), rajas (passion/activity), tamas (inertia/darkness) — and all the states they produce flow from Krishna. Yet the crucial qualification: "na tv aham teṣu te mayi" — I am not in them; they are in Me. Krishna is not bound by what He produces. The ocean is not wet with its own waves. Waves arise in the ocean, are made of ocean, yet the ocean's nature transcends any particular wave. The divine sustains and permeates the guṇas but is not conditioned by them.

# 7.13
short: Deluded by these three states born of the guṇas, this whole world does not know Me — the imperishable, the one beyond them.
long: The great irony: the very guṇas that manifest the divine become the veil that hides it. Sattvic states make people religious but attached to virtue; rajasic states drive them toward power and achievement; tamasic states dull awareness entirely. None of these lead directly to knowing Krishna because all three are finite, conditioned. "Ataḥ param avyayam" — the imperishable beyond them. The world pursues the waves and misses the ocean entirely.

# 7.14
short: This My divine māyā composed of the three guṇas is difficult to cross. Those who take refuge in Me alone cross over this māyā.
long: The famous māyā verse. "Daivī hy eṣā guṇamayī mama māyā" — My divine māyā made of the guṇas. Duratyayā — extremely difficult to cross over. Yet the path is clear: "mām eva ye prapadyante māyām etāṃ taranti te" — those who take refuge in Me alone cross it. Not through intellectual effort, not through ritual, not through renunciation — but through surrender to Krishna. Refuge (prapatti) bypasses the trap of guṇa-consciousness by transcending its frame entirely.

# 7.15
short: The evildoers, the deluded, the lowest of humans, those whose knowledge is destroyed by māyā, and those with a demonic nature — these four do not take refuge in Me.
long: Four types who do not surrender: duṣkṛtina (evil-doers — those who harm others, drowned in tamas); mūḍha (the deluded — those blinded by ignorance); narādhama (lowest of humans — those who squander human birth on pure sense-gratification); and those of āsurī svabhāva (demonic nature — those who deny the divine and exploit others). The key marker: "jñānaṃ yasyāpahriyate māyayā" — whose knowledge is stolen by māyā. The problem is epistemological; the solution is grace.

# 7.16

# 7.17
short: Of these, the jñānī who is ever steadfast and devoted to the One is the best. I am supremely dear to the jñānī and the jñānī is dear to Me.
long: Among the four, the jñānī stands apart — "nitya-yukta eka-bhaktiḥ" — always unified, devoted to the One alone. Why is the jñānī supreme? Because the jñānī knows: there is nothing else to seek. The ārta wants relief; arthārthī wants prosperity; jijñāsu wants knowledge — all seek something else through Krishna. The jñānī seeks only Krishna. "Priyo hi jñānino'tyartham ahaṃ sa ca mama priyaḥ" — I am supremely dear to the jñānī, and the jñānī is dear to Me. Mutual love, not transactional.

# 7.18
short: Noble indeed are all of these, but the jñānī I consider as My very self — for the steadfast-souled jñānī is established in Me alone as the supreme goal.
long: A remarkable statement: "Udārāḥ sarva evaite" — all four are noble. Krishna does not dismiss the ārta or arthārthī. Yet the jñānī is uniquely the "ātmaiva me matam" — My very Self, in My view. How? Because the jñānī has no separate agenda — their being is entirely in Krishna. "Āsthitaḥ sa hi yuktātmā mām evānuttamāṃ gatim" — the jñānī, with concentrated self, is established in Me as the supreme goal. There is no "other"; the jñānī and Krishna have become functionally identical.

# 7.19

# 7.24
short: The unintelligent think I, the unmanifest, have come into manifestation. They do not know My supreme nature, which is immutable and utterly supreme.
long: A common misunderstanding: treating Krishna as simply a form among forms — the unmanifest that happened to become manifest. "Avyaktaṃ vyaktim āpannaṃ manyante mām abuddhayaḥ" — the unintelligent think the unmanifest has taken on manifestation (as if it's a temporary change of state). They miss "param bhāvam avyayam" — My supreme immutable nature. Krishna's manifestation in form does not exhaust or limit the unmanifest absolute. The form is real; it does not contain the formless. This is the paradox of divine incarnation.

# 7.27
short: By the delusion of the pairs of opposites arising from desire and aversion, all beings are led into confusion at birth, O Bharata.
long: The mechanism of cosmic delusion: "icchā-dveṣa-samutthena dvandva-mohena" — by the delusion of pairs-of-opposites arising from desire (icchā) and aversion (dveṣa). All beings are born into dvandva-moha — the trance of duality. Hot/cold, pleasure/pain, gain/loss — each pair locks consciousness into a ping-pong of reaction, covering the unified ground. This begins at birth itself ("sarga") — it is structural to embodied existence in the guṇa-world, not a personal moral failure.

# 7.28
short: But those of meritorious deeds whose sin has ended — freed from the delusion of pairs of opposites — worship Me with firm resolve.
long: The exit from dvandva-moha: "yeṣāṃ tv anta-gataṃ pāpam" — those whose sins have come to an end. Not righteousness by perfection but by completion of karma — the exhaustion of reactive momentum. "Dvandva-moha-nirmuktāḥ" — freed from the delusion of dualities. Then: "bhajante māṃ dṛḍha-vratāḥ" — worship Me with firm resolve. Purified awareness naturally gravitates toward the divine. The "punya" (merit) of past good action makes the vision clearer, the seeking steadier.

# 7.29
short: Those who strive for liberation from old age and death, taking refuge in Me — they know Brahman completely, the Adhyātma, and all of karma.
long: The comprehensive fruit of taking refuge: "jarā-maraṇa-mokṣāya mām āśritya yatanti ye" — those who take refuge in Me striving for liberation from aging and death. "Te brahma tad viduḥ kṛtsnam adhyātmaṃ karma cākhilam" — they know Brahman fully, know the Adhyātma (the inner self, the individual reality), and know all of karma completely. Refuge in the divine doesn't produce ignorance — it produces complete knowledge. The surrender opens all understanding simultaneously.

# 7.30
short: Those who know Me as Adhibhūta, Adhidaiva, and Adhiyajña — with steadied mind, they know Me even at the time of death.
long: The chapter closes with three mysterious terms that will be explained in ch.8: Adhibhūta (the divine principle in matter/beings), Adhidaiva (the divine principle in the gods/senses), Adhiyajña (the divine principle in sacrifice/action). Those who know Krishna in these three modes — "prayāṇa-kāle'pi māṃ te viduḥ" — even at the moment of death they know Me. The final thought of a well-aimed life determines its trajectory beyond. Ch.8 will begin with Arjuna's questions about exactly these terms.
