# 1.1
short: Dhṛtarāṣṭra asks Sañjaya: what did my people and the Pāṇḍavas do when gathered at Kurukṣetra, the field of righteousness, eager for battle?
long: The blind king Dhṛtarāṣṭra opens the Gītā with one question that reveals his anxiety — he calls Kurukṣetra "dharmakṣetra," the field of dharma, perhaps fearing its sacred influence might turn the tide against his sons. The very first word "dharma" sets the theme of the entire Gītā. He asks Sañjaya, who was blessed by Vyāsa with divine sight, to report everything.

The word "dhīmatā" (wise/intelligent) is acknowledgment of a formidable foe.

# 1.10
short: Our army protected by Bhīṣma is unlimited; theirs protected by Bhīma is limited — thus the assessment is different.
long: Duryodhana compares the two armies and concludes his is superior. But the reverse reading is also possible: he says "aparyāptam" (unlimited/insufficient) for his side and "paryāptam" (limited/sufficient) for theirs — the Sanskrit is deliberately ambiguous. Commentators debate this, with some reading it as Duryodhana's hidden anxiety breaking through even as he tries to be confident.

# 1.26

long: The seeing begins. In one sweeping look, Arjuna beholds not enemies but relationships — the entire web of his life arrayed against him. Fathers, teachers, uncles, brothers, sons: this is not an army, it is his world. The Gītā records this moment of recognition because it is the moment when abstract war becomes personal grief, and personal grief opens the door to philosophy.
