# 3.1
short: Arjuna said: O Janārdana, if you consider that wisdom is superior to action, why do you urge me to engage in this terrible action?
long: Arjuna heard two things in chapter 2 that appear contradictory: Kṛṣṇa praised the wisdom of the sthitaprajña (who transcends action) and also urged Arjuna to fight. The question is genuine: if knowledge is higher, why act? This is the central problem of chapter 3 — the relationship between jñāna (knowledge) and karma (action).

# 3.2
short: With this seemingly contradictory speech you are confusing my understanding. Tell me conclusively what is truly good for me.
long: Arjuna feels the contradition as a real cognitive burden — his mind is bewildered (buddhi-mohita). He asks for a single decisive answer, not a both/and. The word "niścitya" (conclusively/with certainty) echoes his request in 2.7 for certainty. He wants clarity, not philosophical nuance. Kṛṣṇa's answer will explain why the apparent contradiction is resolved.

# 3.3
short: The Blessed Lord said: O sinless one, two paths have been taught in this world from the beginning — the path of knowledge for the reflective, and the path of action for the active.
long: The crucial answer: there are two legitimate paths, not one. Sāṅkhya-yoga (the path of discriminative knowledge) for the jñāninaḥ (the contemplative, philosophical type). Karma-yoga (the path of action) for the yoginaḥ (the active type). Neither is wrong; they suit different natures. The contradiction Arjuna perceived was actually a recognition of human diversity.

# 3.4
short: Not by abstaining from actions does a person reach the state of action-lessness; and not by renunciation alone does one attain perfection.
long: Kṛṣṇa pre-empts the obvious misunderstanding: since knowledge is praised, just stop all action! No. Inaction is not the same as transcendence. Simply sitting still while the mind churns desires and plans is not naiskarmya (freedom from action). True actionlessness comes from inner transformation, not external cessation.

# 3.5
short: Indeed, no one can ever remain without acting even for a moment; for all are helplessly driven to action by the qualities born of nature (guṇas).
long: The fundamental insight of chapter 3: action is unavoidable. Every being acts constantly — even the act of refraining from external action involves internal mental action. The guṇas (sattva, rajas, tamas) are the active constituents of nature (prakṛti), and they act through every being. Nobody is truly inactive. The choice is not action vs. inaction, but conscious vs. unconscious action.

# 3.6
short: One who restrains the organs of action but who dwells on sense-objects with the mind — such a deluded person is called a hypocrite.
long: The hypocrite (mithyācāra) is defined: external restraint with internal churning. The body sits still but the mind runs after all its desires. This is worse than honest engagement — it combines the costs of abstinence with the bondage of desire. Kṛṣṇa is clearing space for Karma Yoga: the right path is honest, wholehearted action, not suppressed action.

# 3.7
short: But one who, controlling the senses with the mind, engages the organs of action without attachment — such a person is superior, O Arjuna.
long: The contrast to the hypocrite: controlling the senses through the mind (not just the body), then engaging actively and without attachment (anāsaktaḥ). This is true yoga — the integration of inner discipline and outer action. The organs of action (karmendriyāṇi) are not suppressed but directed. This person is viśiṣyate — distinguished/superior.

# 3.8
short: Perform your prescribed duty, for action is superior to inaction. Even the maintenance of your body cannot be accomplished without action.
long: The practical ground level: niyatam karma (prescribed, obligatory duty) must be done. Why? Because inaction — even for a supposedly contemplative person — cannot be sustained: the body must be fed, cared for, maintained. And maintenance of the body requires action. The argument for action is not heroic but biological: life demands activity.

# 3.9
short: This world is bound by actions other than those performed for the sake of yajña (sacrifice). O son of Kuntī, perform action for that purpose, free from attachment.
long: The concept of yajña (sacrifice/offering) enters. All action that is selfish (not yajña) creates karma bondage. But action performed as yajña — as an offering, as a service, for a higher purpose than one's own gain — does not bind. The key distinction: who is the action for? The Gītā redefines yajña beyond ritual sacrifice to any action done in the spirit of offering.

# 3.10
short: In the beginning, having created beings along with sacrifice, the Creator said: By this you shall multiply; let this (yajña) be your wish-fulfilling cow.
long: A cosmogonic claim: creation itself was set up as a sacrificial exchange. Prajāpati (the Creator) created beings along with yajña (sacrifice) and declared them interdependent — through sacrifice you flourish. "Kāmadhuk" — the wish-fulfilling cow — suggests yajña is not mere obligation but the mechanism by which human wellbeing is sustained. Cooperation with the cosmic order, not exploitation of it.

# 3.11
short: Nourish the gods with this sacrifice, and let the gods nourish you; thus nourishing each other, you shall attain the highest good.
long: The mutual-nourishment model: human beings nourish the divine powers (devas) through yajña; the devas in turn nourish human beings through rain, harvest, fertility. This is the ancient Vedic vision of cosmic reciprocity. The word "parasparaṃ bhāvayantaḥ" — nourishing each other — extends beyond human-divine to all relationships. The highest good (param śreyas) arises from this interdependence.

# 3.12
short: Nourished by sacrifice, the gods will give you the desired enjoyments. One who enjoys their gifts without offering in return is indeed a thief.
long: The devas (cosmic powers) give prosperity — rain, crops, health — but only in response to yajña. One who takes these gifts without giving back is a steya (thief). This is a radical ethical claim: all enjoyment that doesn't flow from a spirit of offering is a form of stealing from the cosmic order. The consumer who only takes is spiritually bankrupt.

# 3.13
short: The saintly persons who eat the remnants of sacrifice are freed from all sins; but those who cook only for their own sake — truly they eat sin.
long: The yajñaśeṣa (remnants of sacrifice) is transformed by the act of offering. What was selfishly taken becomes sacred when offered first and shared. Those who cook only for themselves (kevalaṃ te'ghnaṃ bhuñjate) eat sin — not because the food is tainted but because the orientation is wrong. This principle can be extended: any action done purely for oneself becomes spiritually harmful.

# 3.14
short: Beings are born from food; food is born from rain; rain comes from sacrifice; sacrifice is born from action.
long: The cosmic chain is laid out: beings → food → rain → sacrifice → action. Action (karma) sustains sacrifice (yajña) sustains rain (parjanya) sustains food (anna) sustains life (bhūtāni). This is the Vedic ecology: human ritual action participates in the natural cycle. Refusing to act in this chain is to break it — not just ethically but cosmologically.

# 3.15
short: Know that action arises from Brahman; and Brahman arises from the imperishable (Akṣara). Therefore the all-pervading Brahman is always established in sacrifice.
long: The chain extends upward: action → Brahman (here meaning the Veda/cosmic order) → Akṣara (the Imperishable, the Absolute). The entire cosmological cycle (3.14-15) originates in and returns to the Imperishable. Sacrifice participates in the eternal order — it is not a human invention but a cosmic principle. This grounds yajña in metaphysics, not just in social custom.

# 3.16
short: One who does not follow this wheel thus set in motion — who lives in sin and enjoys the senses — such a person lives in vain, O Arjuna.
long: "Evaṃ pravartitaṃ cakram" — the wheel thus set in motion — is the cosmic cycle of sacrifice just described. The person who does not participate in this wheel (anuvartayati), who lives selfishly, sins (agha-āyuḥ) and indulges the senses, lives meaninglessly (mogham). A human life that does not contribute to the cosmic order is wasted. This is not a threat but a cosmological fact.

# 3.17
short: But for that person who rejoices in the Self alone, who is satisfied with the Self, and is content in the Self — for such a person there is nothing that need be done.
long: The exception: the person who has realized the Self (ātmārāma — one who rejoices in the Self) is released from the cosmic obligation. Their action or inaction no longer has consequences in the same sense, because they have transcended the level at which yajña operates. They have arrived at the source — the Akṣara. For them, everything is already whole. This is the jivanmukta.

# 3.18
short: For such a person there is nothing to gain by actions done, nor anything to lose by actions not done; nor does such a person depend on any being for anything.
long: Complete freedom from the calculus of gain and loss. The ātmārāma has no "naiva tasya kṛtenārthaḥ" (no purpose served by action) and no "nākṛtena kaścana" (nothing lost by inaction). And crucially: "na cāsya sarva-bhūteṣu kaścid artha-vyapāśrayaḥ" — no dependence on any being for anything. This is absolute self-sufficiency — not selfishness but the overflow of fullness.

# 3.19

For him (and for most people), the instruction is: do your duty (kāryam karma) without attachment (asakta). And this path of detached action is itself the road to the Supreme (param āpnoti). Action and liberation are not opposed; detached action is liberation in progress.

long: Leadership by example. The great person (śreṣṭha) sets the standard that others imitate — "yad yad ācarati" (whatever one does), that becomes the model. Kṛṣṇa is deploying this argument for Arjuna specifically: as a warrior-hero, Arjuna's behavior will be emulated by ordinary soldiers and citizens. If he flees, others flee. If he acts with wisdom, others learn.

long: If Kṛṣṇa, the supreme being, were to become inactive, everyone would follow suit — since the great set the standard (verse 3.21). The world would grind to halt. "Tandrī" — sloth, torpor — is the danger. Even the enlightened must act, because their example creates the template others live by. Self-realization does not exempt one from social responsibility.

# 3.25

long: The model for the wise person: do exactly what the ordinary person does externally, but with a different inner orientation. The unwise act from attachment (sakta); the wise act without attachment (asakta), from care for the world (lokasaṃgraham). The action looks the same from outside; only the motivation differs. This is the Gītā's answer to the question: why do the enlightened continue to act?

The teacher does not confuse; the teacher leads by example in a way that guides without uprooting.

# 3.27
short: All actions are performed by the qualities of nature. But one deluded by ego thinks "I am the doer."
long: The metaphysical basis of non-attachment: in reality, it is the guṇas (qualities of prakṛti/nature) that act. The ego (ahaṃkāra) falsely claims "I am doing this." The wise person sees through this claim. This shifts the question: who is really acting? Not the true Self, but the guṇas playing themselves out through the body-mind. The Self watches without being the doer.

# 3.28
short: But one who knows the truth about the division of qualities and actions, O mighty-armed, understanding that the qualities move among the qualities, is not attached.
long: The knowing person (tattva-vit) understands that guṇas interact with guṇas — the senses (which are guṇas) encounter sense-objects (also guṇas); the mind (guṇa) processes experiences. None of this involves the true Self. Understanding this, the wise person does not attach themselves to the outcomes. They watch the guṇa-play without claiming ownership of it.

# 3.29
short: Those deluded by the qualities of nature become attached to the actions of those qualities. The person of perfect knowledge should not disturb these fools of imperfect knowledge.
long: The same principle as verse 3.26 but from the metaphysical angle: people of limited understanding are naturally attached to the guṇa-driven actions — that is their level. The fully knowing person (kṛtsna-vit) should not disturb (na vicālayet) these people of partial knowledge (manda-kṛtsna-vit). Lead them gently, not by ripping away their framework.

# 3.32
short: But those who, out of envy, do not practice this teaching of Mine and ridicule it — know them to be fools, deluded in all their knowledge, and doomed.

Envy prevents learning; mockery closes the door. The "knowledge" of the mocker is actually deeper delusion. This is a sharp warning but also psychologically accurate: cynicism is its own prison.

# 3.33
short: Even a person of knowledge acts in accordance with their own nature; all beings follow their own nature. What will restraint accomplish?
long: A profound admission of limits: "sadṛśam ceṣṭate svasyāḥ prakṛteḥ" — everyone acts in accordance with their own nature. Even a wise person. Nature (svabhāva, prakṛti) is extraordinarily powerful. What will mere repression (nigraha) accomplish? This verse is not fatalistic — Kṛṣṇa does not say "so don't try." Rather, transformation must go deeper than surface restraint.

# 3.34
short: Attraction and aversion are fixed with regard to the sense-objects of each sense. One should not come under the power of these two — they are, indeed, one's enemies.
long: The two primal pulls — rāga (attraction/desire) and dveṣa (aversion/hatred) — are seated in the sensory apparatus. They are like permanent fixtures, arising automatically. But the instruction is not to surrender to them — "na vaśam āgacchet" — do not fall under their control. They are enemies (paripanthinau). Understanding them as enemies does not mean fighting them blindly but not submitting to their automatic dominion.

# 3.35
short: Better is one's own duty, though imperfectly performed, than the duty of another even if well performed. Death in performing one's own duty is preferable; the duty of another is fraught with danger.
long: The celebrated svadharma principle: performing one's own duty imperfectly (svanuṣṭhitat) is better than performing another's duty perfectly (su-anuṣṭhitat). "Paradharmā bhayāvahaḥ" — another's duty is fraught with fear/danger. This applies to Arjuna directly: his dharma is that of a warrior, not a scholar or renunciant. Attempting to adopt another's role (even a "higher" one) without the nature to sustain it leads to failure and loss.

# 3.38
short: As fire is enveloped by smoke, as a mirror by dust, and as an embryo by the womb, so is knowledge enveloped by this (desire).
long: Three analogies for how desire (kāma) veils knowledge (jñāna): smoke veils fire (they coexist but the fire is obscured); dust veils a mirror (the reflective capacity is blocked); the womb envelops the embryo (the child is enclosed). The analogies suggest different degrees of concealment. Desire doesn't destroy knowledge — it covers it. The covering can be removed. This is the basis for hope in spiritual practice.

# 3.39
short: O son of Kuntī, by this constant enemy in the form of desire, knowledge is covered — and this fire of desire is difficult to satisfy.

And it is described as kāma-rūpa (in the form of desire), durniveśa (difficult to be content, insatiable). The fire of desire grows with feeding. Knowledge (jñāna) — the counter-weapon — is covered by this insatiable force. The practitioner must understand the depth of the enemy before developing the counterforce.

This language of warfare is intentional: kāma must be actively engaged and overcome, not just philosophically understood.

# 3.42
short: They say the senses are superior to the sense-objects; the mind is superior to the senses; the intellect is superior to the mind; but higher than the intellect is the Self.
long: A hierarchy of faculties: viṣaya (sense-objects) < indriyāṇi (senses) < manaḥ (mind) < buddhiḥ (intellect) < ātmā (Self). Each level has power to govern the level below it. The strategy against kāma follows this hierarchy — regulate the senses with the mind, regulate the mind with the intellect, and ultimately, establish the intellect in the Self. The battle has a strategy.

# 3.43
short: Thus knowing the Self to be higher than the intellect, O mighty-armed, strengthen the lower self by the higher self, and slay the enemy in the form of desire, which is so difficult to conquer.
long: The culminating instruction of chapter 3: knowing the Self (Ātman) to be higher than even the buddhi (intellect), use the buddhi (the higher self that reflects the Ātman) to regulate the lower self (the personality driven by kāma). "Jahi śatrum" — slay this enemy. "Mahābāho" — the mighty-armed one — is reminded that the real battle is internal. The weapons are wisdom and self-governance, not swords.
