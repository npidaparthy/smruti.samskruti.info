# 10.1
short: Krishna asks Arjuna to again hear His supreme word — which He will speak for Arjuna's benefit out of love.
long: Chapter 10 opens with an intimate note of love. Krishna addresses Arjuna as "mahābāho" (mighty-armed) — an honor — and as "prīyamāṇāya" (one dear to Me). This combination of power and love defines the ideal spiritual relationship: the student is both capable and beloved. The phrase "bhūya eva" (once again) signals that what follows is not entirely new but a deepening of the teaching already given. The repetition is not redundancy but the pedagogy of internalization.

# 10.2
short: Neither gods nor great sages know My origin — for I am the origin of them all.
long: This verse establishes a crucial asymmetry: the beings who are most revered in the cosmic hierarchy — the gods (devas) and the great sages (maharṣis) — are themselves products of the divine creative power. A product cannot fully know its cause by looking only at itself. The gods know many things, but they cannot know the source of their own existence by introspection or perception, because the Divine is beyond both. This is not a boast but a metaphysical statement about the structure of knowing.

# 10.3
short: He who knows Me as unborn, beginningless, and the great Lord of worlds — among mortals, he alone is undeluded and freed from all evils.
long: The path to liberation begins with correct cognition — knowing what the Divine truly is. Most human conceptions of God carry the shape of human experience: a being who was born, who grew, who has a cause, who lives within time. To know Krishna as "aja" (unborn) and "anādi" (beginningless) is to see past all anthropomorphisms. He is not a temporal being but the ground of temporality itself. This knowledge is not merely theological — it is liberating ("sa asmāt sarva-pāpebhyo mokṣyate").

# 10.4
short: Intelligence, knowledge, freedom from delusion, forbearance, truth, control of senses and mind, joy and sorrow, birth and death, fear and fearlessness —
long: This and the next verse list twenty qualities that arise in beings — all of which flow from Krishna. The list bridges inner virtues (buddhi, jñāna, amoha) with outer circumstances (birth, death, fear, fearlessness). By placing subjective qualities like "joy and sorrow" alongside metaphysical realities like "birth and death," Krishna shows that nothing in experience — psychological or existential — falls outside the divine order. All qualities are divine gifts, even the ones that seem painful (fear, sorrow).

# 10.5
short: Non-violence, equanimity, contentment, austerity, charity, fame, and infamy — all these diverse qualities of beings arise from Me alone.
long: The list continues with ethical qualities (ahiṃsā, samatā), inner states (santoṣa), spiritual practices (tapaḥ), social virtues (dānam), and social standing (kīrti, akīrti). The inclusion of "infamy" (akīrti) alongside "fame" (kīrti) is significant: even negative social states arise from the divine order. This does not excuse immoral behavior — rather, it invites a cosmic perspective that sees even unfavorable conditions as part of the divine unfolding, and frees one from the tyranny of other people's opinions.

# 10.6
short: The seven great sages and also the four Manus of old were born from My mind — from them all beings in the world were born.
long: The cosmic genealogy is revealed: the seven primordial sages (Marīci, Atri, Aṅgiras, Pulaha, Kratu, Pulastya, Vasiṣṭha) and the four Manus (progenitors of human races in the four world-ages) all arose from Krishna's cosmic mind. They are not independent creative forces but expressions of divine intentionality. "Manasā jātāḥ" — born from Mind — suggests a creative process more like thought than physical generation. All of humanity traces its lineage, through the Manus, to this one divine creative impulse.

One who truly understands that the radiance of all beautiful and powerful things in creation is a ray of Krishna's glory — that person's meditation naturally becomes steady and unified. The doubt-free quality of this realization is significant: it is not tentative conviction but direct recognition.

# 10.8
short: I am the source of everything — from Me all proceeds — knowing this, the wise worship Me with devotion.
long: This is one of the most direct theological statements in the Gita: "ahaṃ sarvasya prabhavo" — I am the source of all. Not merely a contributor to creation, not one cause among many, but the ultimate ground of all that exists. The response of the wise (buddhā) is not philosophical satisfaction but bhajana — devotional worship with a knowing heart (bhāva-samanvitāḥ, meaning not ritual alone but accompanied by inner feeling and understanding). Wisdom that does not flower into devotion is incomplete.

# 10.9
short: Their minds fixed on Me, their lives surrendered to Me, enlightening each other, speaking of Me always — they are content and rejoice.
long: A community of devotees is described here — not isolated individuals but a sangha (community) of practitioners who mutually strengthen one another's love for the Divine. "Kathayantaś ca māṃ nityam" — speaking of Me always — refers to kīrtana (devotional singing, storytelling about the Lord), satsaṅga (holy company), and pravacana (discourse). In such company, enlightenment is not a solitary achievement but a shared unfolding. Their contentment (tuṣyanti) and joy (ramanti) are the natural byproducts of their God-absorbed lives.

# 10.10
short: To those ever-devoted who worship Me with love, I give the yoga of discrimination by which they come to Me.
long: The grace of the teacher is explicitly promised here. Buddhi-yoga — the yoga of discriminative intelligence — is a gift that flows between the devoted student and the divine Teacher. The phrase "dadāmi" (I give) emphasizes that this wisdom is not earned in the sense of a commercial transaction but is a gracious overflow from the Teacher's abundance. The condition is sincere devotion (bhajatāṃ prīti-pūrvakam — worshipping with love). Love opens the channel; discrimination fills it; the destination is union with Krishna.

# 10.11
short: Out of compassion for them, I, dwelling in their hearts, destroy with the shining lamp of knowledge the darkness born of ignorance.
long: The divine is not merely a response to devotion but is proactively compassionate (anukampārtham). The image of the blazing lamp of knowledge (jñāna-dīpena bhāsvatā) destroying darkness born of ignorance (ajñāna-jam tamaḥ) is a meditation image used widely in Vedāntic practice. The darkness of ignorance is not punished or argued away — it is simply illuminated. Ātman is self-luminous; ignorance is the mere absence of correct seeing. When the lamp is lit from within (from the divine dwelling in the heart), the darkness cannot survive.

# 10.13
short: All the sages declare this — Nārada, Asita, Devala, and Vyāsa — and now You Yourself are telling me this.
long: Arjuna grounds his recognition in the testimony of the great sages. This is an important epistemological move in the Indian tradition: individual insight gains authority when it aligns with the testimony of the great seers (āptavacana). Nārada — the divine sage of devotion; Asita and Devala — ancient Vedic seers; and Vyāsa — the very author of the Mahābhārata who gave the Gita its literary form — all declare the same truth. And now Krishna confirms it directly. Revelation and tradition converge.

# 10.14
short: I accept all You tell me as truth, O Keśava — neither gods nor demons can know Your manifestation, O Lord.
long: This verse shows Arjuna's full acceptance — not merely intellectual assent but śraddhā (faith rooted in relationship). "Sarvam etad ṛtam manye" — I consider all this to be true — is a complete yielding of the skeptical faculty to the authority of the teacher. The admission that even gods and demons cannot know the Lord's manifestation echoes 10.2 — Arjuna is now aligning himself with the cosmic truth that the divine origin is unknowable except through grace. His faith is earned, not blind.

# 10.15
short: You alone know Yourself through Your own Self, O Supreme Puruṣa — creator of all beings — Lord of beings — God of gods — Lord of the universe.
long: This verse arrives at the final epistemological boundary: ultimate self-knowledge belongs only to the Supreme. Even the greatest sages know the Divine only as it reveals itself to them — not in its totality. The Divine knows itself completely, through itself, without any mediation. Arjuna addresses Krishna with four great titles: Bhūta-Bhāvana (creator of all beings), Bhūteśa (Lord of beings), Deva-Deva (God of gods), Jagat-Pate (Lord of the universe). Each reveals a different axis of divine sovereignty.

# 10.16
short: Please tell me fully of Your divine manifestations by which You pervade all these worlds and remain in them.
long: Having expressed faith and recognized the divine identity, Arjuna now requests a practical teaching: tell me about the vibhūtis — the specific, concrete manifestations through which I can recognize You in the world. This is a devotee's practical question: given that You are everywhere, how do I see You? The answer will train Arjuna's (and the reader's) perception to recognize the sacred in the magnificent — in the brilliance of the sun, the speed of the wind, the strength of the mountains. Vibhūti-yoga is a meditation practice disguised as cosmology.

# 10.18
short: Tell me again in detail of Your yoga and manifestations, O Janārdana — I am not satiated hearing Your nectar-like words.
long: "Nāsti tattor amṛtam" — the divine teaching is compared to amṛta (nectar, ambrosia). Just as one who has tasted nectar wants more, the devotee who has truly heard sacred teaching is never satisfied — not because the teaching is incomplete but because each repetition opens a new depth. Arjuna's hunger is a sign of authentic receptivity. The great spiritual teachings of India were often structured for repetition: the student asks the teacher to elaborate, to repeat, to say it again and again, because each hearing at a deeper level of readiness yields a different understanding.

# 10.19

The vibhūtis to be described are the most prominent (pradhānyataḥ) — the peaks visible above the clouds of ordinary perception — but the full catalog would be endless, because every particle of creation is a manifestation of the Divine. This limitation is not a failure of the teacher but a gracious concession to the student's finite mind.

# 10.20
short: I am the Self, O Guḍākeśa, dwelling in the hearts of all beings — I am the beginning, middle, and end of all beings.
long: The most important vibhūti is stated first and last: the ātman itself. "Gudākeśa sarvabhūtāśaya-sthitaḥ" — dwelling in the heart of all beings. Every other vibhūti is a specific, outer manifestation; this one is the innermost, universal one. By saying "I am the beginning, middle, and end," Krishna takes responsibility for the entire arc of existence — birth, life, and death are not accidents but divine expressions. The heart (āśaya) is the deepest seat of consciousness where the divine meets the individual.

Among the Maruts (storm winds), He is Marīci — the most luminous, the speed of light itself. Among the stars, He is the moon — the lord of the night sky who regulates time and seasons. Each choice is the most glorious, most powerful representative of its category.

# 10.22
short: Among the Vedas I am the Sāmaveda — among the gods I am Indra — among the senses I am the mind — among beings I am consciousness.
long: Among the four Vedas, the Sāmaveda is chosen — not because it is superior in content but because it is the most musical, the most devotional, the one that most directly lifts the heart through melody. Among the thirty-three gods (of the Vedic pantheon), Indra is the king — the wielder of the thunderbolt, the lord of rain and victory. Among the eleven senses, the mind (manas) is singled out because it is the coordinating faculty through which all other sense experiences are processed. Among beings (bhūteṣu), the divine vibhūti is chetanā — pure consciousness, the light by which all beings are aware.

# 10.42
short: But what need is there for all this elaborate knowledge, O Arjuna — I sustain this entire universe with a single fraction of Myself and yet remain.
long: The concluding verse is magnificent in its understatement. After 40 verses of cosmic cataloguing — all the Vedas, the sages, the gods, the powers, the excellences of creation — Krishna sweeps it all aside with serene economy: "atha vā bahunaitena" — or rather, what is all this elaborate knowledge for? The entire universe — with all its billions of galaxies and the unimaginable diversity of life — is sustained by just a single fraction (ekāṃśena) of the Divine, which then "remains" (tiṣṭhate) — meaning the full extent of the Divine is not depleted or absorbed by creation. The creation is a tiny ripple on an infinite ocean.
