# 5.1
short: Arjuna said: O Kṛṣṇa, you praise renunciation of actions and then yoga (of action). Tell me decisively which of these two is better.
long: Arjuna revives the question from chapter 3: if both action and renunciation lead to liberation, which is superior? He wants a definitive answer. The chapter will argue that external renunciation without inner transformation is inferior to karma yoga, and that ultimately both paths converge for the self-realized.

# 5.2
short: The Blessed Lord said: Both renunciation and yoga of action lead to liberation. But of the two, karma yoga is superior to sannyāsa (renunciation of action).
long: Kṛṣṇa gives his verdict: karma yoga (yoga of action) is better than mere sannyāsa (external renunciation). Both lead to the supreme (niḥśreyasam). But karma yoga is "viśiṣyate" — distinguished/superior. The reason, as the next verses clarify: genuine renunciation is an inner state, not an external posture, and karma yoga more reliably cultivates that inner state.

# 5.3
short: One who neither hates nor desires (is free from dualities) is to be known as a constant renunciant. Free from duality, that person is easily freed from bondage, O mighty-armed.
long: The definition of a true sannyāsī: not one who wears ochre robes but one who "neither hates nor desires" (dvandvas). "Nitya-sannyāsī" — always a renunciant. And the result: "sukhaṃ bandhāt pramucyate" — is easily freed from bondage. External renunciation without this inner non-dual quality is not real renunciation.

# 5.4
short: Children, not the wise, speak of Sāṅkhya and yoga as different. One who is properly established in either obtains the fruits of both.
long: "Bālāḥ" (children/the immature) see Sāṅkhya (knowledge path / renunciation) and yoga (karma yoga) as opposites. But the wise (paṇḍitāḥ) know they are not. One who achieves either achieves both. Why? Because both paths, if pursued fully, lead to the same inner realization — the transcendence of ego-doership. The apparent difference is pedagogical, not ontological.

# 5.5
short: The state reached by Sāṅkhyas is also attained by yogis. One who sees Sāṅkhya and yoga as one — that person truly sees.
long: The convergence thesis: Sāṅkhya (jñāna, the path of knowledge and renunciation) and yoga (karma yoga, the path of action) lead to the same goal. "Yaḥ paśyati sa paśyati" — the one who sees this unity truly sees. The apparent divergence between the two paths is the starting point; the goal is the same. This is one of the Gītā's integrative moves.

# 5.6
short: Renunciation (sannyāsa) is indeed hard to attain without yoga (of action); the sage yoked to yoga attains Brahman quickly.
long: Without yoga (karma yoga's discipline), sannyāsa is very difficult: "duḥkham āptum ayogataḥ." The yoga-yukta muni (sage yoked to yoga) reaches Brahman "na cireṇa" — soon, without delay. This provides the practical case for karma yoga: it is the more accessible and effective path for most people, especially at the outset of spiritual life.

# 5.7
short: One who is yoked to yoga, whose self is purified, whose self is controlled, whose senses are conquered, whose self becomes all beings — even acting, one is not tainted.
long: The qualities of the karma yogi who acts without being tainted: yoga-yukta (yoked to yoga), viśuddha-ātmā (purified self), vijita-ātmā (self-controlled), jita-indriya (senses conquered), sarva-bhūta-ātma-bhūtātmā (self having become the self of all beings — universal empathy/identification). For such a person, "kurvann api na lipyate" — even while acting, not tainted. The inner quality, not the action itself, determines bondage.

# 5.9
short: — speaking, letting go, seizing, opening and closing the eyes: "The senses move among their objects" — one who is convinced of this.
long: The continuation: pralapan (speaking), visṛjan (letting go/excreting), gṛhṇan (seizing/grasping), unmīṣan-nimīṣan (opening and closing eyes). "Indriyāṇīndriyārtheṣu vartante iti dhārayan" — "The senses are moving among their objects" — holding this conviction. The yogi's certainty is: "I (the Self) am not doing any of this; the senses are acting through their objects." This conviction does not destroy behavior — it liberates the one who holds it.

# 5.10
short: One who acts placing all actions in Brahman, abandoning attachment — is not tainted by sin, just as a lotus leaf is not wetted by water.
long: The image of the lotus: a lotus grows in water, rooted in mud, but its leaf repels water — unaffected. Similarly the karma yogi "acts in Brahman" (brahmaṇy ādhāya karmāṇi) with saṅga-tyaktvā (attachment abandoned). Sin (pāpa) does not adhere. The lotus image will appear again in the tradition as the model of engaged non-attachment.

# 5.11
short: Yogis act with body, mind, intelligence, and even with senses alone — abandoning attachment — for self-purification.
long: The karma yogi uses all five instruments: body (kāya), mind (manas), intelligence (buddhi), and senses (indriya) — all engage in action, but always "tyaktvā saṅgam" — with attachment abandoned. The purpose is "ātma-śuddhaye" — self-purification. Action is not abandoned; the inner grip on action is abandoned. The instrument acts; the inner self watches, purifying.

# 5.12
short: A yoked (disciplined) person, having abandoned the fruit of action, attains lasting peace. An undisciplined person, attached to the fruit, is bound by desire-driven action.
long: The pivotal contrast: yuktaḥ (the yoked/disciplined one) abandons karmaphalam (fruit of action) and attains "śāntim āptikām" — lasting peace. Ayuktaḥ (the undisciplined), kāma-kāreṇa (driven by desire), is bound by action. The difference is not the action but the attachment to results. This is the karma yoga formula in one verse.

# 5.13
short: The embodied one, having renounced all actions mentally, dwells happily in the city of nine gates, not acting, not causing action.
long: The "city of nine gates" is the body (two eyes, two ears, two nostrils, mouth, anus, urethra = nine openings). The inner self "dwells happily" (sukham āste) within this city — not acting (na kurvan), not causing action (na kārayan). The Self does not act; the body and its faculties act. The liberated person identifies with the inner resident (the Self), not the city's activities.

# 5.14
short: The Lord creates neither the agency nor the actions of the world, nor the connection with the fruits of action — nature proceeds by its own quality.
long: A crucial metaphysical clarification: the Lord (Prabhu) does not create kartṛtvam (the quality of being an agent) or karmāṇi (actions) in people, nor does he connect karma with its fruits. "Svabhāvas tu pravartate" — nature proceeds by its own quality/power. God is not manipulating karma's cause-effect nexus. The guṇas of prakṛti create both the agent-sense and the action-fruit connection. This relieves the divine of karmic micromanagement.

# 5.15
short: The omnipresent One accepts neither the sin nor the merit of anyone. Knowledge is covered by ignorance — by that, beings are deluded.
long: Why do the ignorant believe God punishes or rewards? Because their knowledge is "āvṛtam ajñānena" — covered by ignorance. In this ignorance, they project human karmic logic onto the divine. The Lord is not a moral accountant collecting sin and virtue. He is "vibhu" — all-pervading and free. This verse is a defense of divine transcendence against anthropomorphic misunderstanding.

# 5.16
short: But for those whose ignorance is destroyed by knowledge of the Self — their knowledge illumines the supreme, like the sun.
long: The reversal: when the ātman's knowledge destroys ajñānam (ignorance), it illumines "tat paraṃ" — the supreme — like the sun (āditya-vat). The analogy is precise: the sun does not create light, it is light. Knowledge of the Self is not acquired — it is what remains when ignorance is removed. Ātman-jñāna is its own illumination.

# 5.21
short: One who has a self not attached to external contact, finding happiness in the Self — that one, having a self yoked to the yoga of Brahman, attains imperishable happiness.
long: Happiness sourced in the Self (ātmani) rather than "bāhya-sparśeṣu" (in external contacts) is imperishable. External pleasures are born of sense-contact (sparśas) and are therefore impermanent — they have a beginning and an end. The yogi who finds ānanda (joy) within attains "akṣayam sukham" — undecaying happiness. This internal sourcing is the practical definition of sukha (happiness) in the Gītā.

# 5.22
short: For pleasures born of contact are indeed wombs of suffering. They have a beginning and an end, O Arjuna; the wise person does not delight in them.
long: The analysis of sensory pleasure: "ye hi saṃsparśajā bhogā" — pleasures born of contact (with sense objects). These are "duḥkha-yonaya" — sources/wombs of suffering. Why? Because they are ādyanta-vantaḥ — they have a beginning and an end. What begins ends. What ends brings suffering by its ending. Therefore "na teṣu ramate budhaḥ" — the wise do not delight in them. Not ascetic rejection — clear-eyed understanding of their nature.

# 5.26
short: For those liberated from desire and anger, of disciplined thought, who know the Self — Brahman-nirvāṇa exists all around.
long: Brahman-nirvāṇa is not a distant destination — it is "samantaḥ" (all around, on every side) for those who qualify. The conditions: kāma-krodha-viyuktānāṃ (freed from desire and anger), yatī-nāṃ (self-restrained), yata-cetasām (controlled in thought), abhijñānam (Self-knowers). For these, liberation is not a future event — it is already present everywhere around them.

# 5.27
short: Shutting out external contacts, with the gaze between the brows, making the prāṇa and apāna equal in the nostrils—
long: (Continues into 5.28) The beginning of a brief meditation instruction: "sparśān" (external contacts — sense impressions) shut out (kṛtvā bahiḥ), gaze "bhruvor antaraṃ" (between the eyebrows — the practice of trikuṭi/ājñā-cakra focus), and "prāṇāpānau samau" (making the outgoing and incoming breath equal in the nostrils). This introduces dhyāna-yoga practice as the culminating discipline.

# 5.28
short: — with senses, mind, and intellect controlled, the sage intent on liberation, freed from desire, fear, and anger — is ever free.
long: The continuation: yata-indriya-mano-buddhiḥ (senses, mind, intellect controlled), mokṣa-parāyaṇaḥ (intent on liberation/highest goal), vīta-icchā-bhaya-krodhaḥ (freed from desire, fear, and anger). Such a sage is "sadā mukta" — always free. The meditation described in 5.27-28 is a compressed summary of the yogic path: physical posture, breath control, sense withdrawal, one-pointed focus.

# 5.29
short: Knowing Me as the enjoyer of sacrifices and austerities, the Great Lord of all worlds, the friend of all beings — one attains peace.
long: The chapter's final verse is a devotional declaration: the one who knows Kṛṣṇa as bhoktāram (the enjoyer/experiencer of yajña and tapas), mahā-Īśvaram (the great Lord of all worlds), suhṛdam (the friend of all beings) — attains śāntim (peace). This verse introduces the devotional dimension that will become central in ch. 7-12. The friend (suhṛd) is a tender word for the cosmic Lord.
