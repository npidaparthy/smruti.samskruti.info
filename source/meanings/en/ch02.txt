# 2.1
short: Sañjaya said: To him who was thus overwhelmed with pity, whose eyes were filled with tears and troubled, and who was despondent, Madhusūdana (Kṛṣṇa) spoke these words.
long: The chapter opens with Arjuna in full collapse — tears, trembling, grief. Sañjaya narrates to Dhṛtarāṣṭra with the compassionate epithet "Madhusūdana" (slayer of demon Madhu), perhaps hinting that Kṛṣṇa will also slay the inner demon of delusion now gripping Arjuna. This sets up the entire Gītā as Kṛṣṇa's response to a human in crisis.

# 2.2
short: The Blessed Lord said: O Arjuna, how has this faint-heartedness come upon you at this crisis? It does not befit a noble man; it will not lead to heaven; it causes disgrace.
long: Kṛṣṇa's very first word is a challenge — not comfort, but a call to self-examination. He frames Arjuna's grief as "klaibya" (unmanliness/impotence), not mere sadness. Three reasons to shake it off: it is unworthy of nobility (anārya), it blocks heaven (asvargyam), and it brings infamy (akīrtikaram). Kṛṣṇa opens with urgency, not philosophy.

# 2.3
short: Do not yield to impotence, O Arjuna. It does not befit you. Shake off faint-heartedness and arise.
long: A brisk, direct command — the shortest of Kṛṣṇa's opening salvos. "Klaibyam" (impotence/unmanliness) is a strong word, and Kṛṣṇa uses it deliberately to sting Arjuna out of self-pity. "Uttara" — arise — will echo at the end of the Gītā (18.73), forming a frame around the entire teaching.

# 2.4
short: Arjuna said: How, O Madhusūdana, shall I fight with arrows against Bhīṣma and Droṇa in battle — they who are worthy of worship?
long: Arjuna shifts from pure grief to a reasoned objection: these men are not enemies, they are elders and teachers who deserve his reverence (pūjā). How can he shoot arrows at those whose feet he should be touching? This is not cowardice but a real moral puzzle — the conflict between duty to fight and duty to revere. The question is genuine.

# 2.5
short: It is better to live by begging than to slay these great-souled teachers. By killing them even for worldly gain, I would only be enjoying blood-stained pleasures.
long: Arjuna makes a stark economic calculation: beggary is preferable to prospering on the earnings of killing one's gurus. "Blood-stained pleasures" (bhuñjīya bhogān rudhira-pradigdhān) is a visceral image — Arjuna foresees that all enjoyments afterward would be morally tainted. The reasoning is heartfelt even if it will be overturned.

# 2.6
short: And we do not know which is preferable for us — to conquer them or to be conquered by them. Even the sons of Dhṛtarāṣṭra, having killed whom we would not wish to live, stand before us.
long: Arjuna now admits uncertainty at the root level: he does not know whether victory or defeat is better. If he kills and wins, what has he gained? If he is killed, perhaps that is preferable. The sons of Dhṛtarāṣṭra are his cousins — killing them would hollow out any victory. The confusion is complete, and Arjuna is ready to become a student.

# 2.7
short: My nature is afflicted by weakness and compassion; my mind is confused about duty. I ask you: tell me with certainty what is good for me — I am your disciple; teach me who has taken refuge in you.
long: This is the pivotal verse — Arjuna formally becomes a student (śiṣya) and surrenders (śaraṇam) to Kṛṣṇa as guru. The three problems are named: kārpaṇya (weakness/miserliness of spirit), dharma-sammūḍha-cetā (mind confused about dharma), and pṛcchāmi (I ask). From confusion comes the question, from the question comes the Gītā.

# 2.8
short: For I do not see what would remove this grief that is drying up my senses, even if I were to obtain an unrivalled and prosperous kingdom on earth or lordship over the gods.
long: Arjuna completes his opening statement: the grief is absolute, not conditional. Even sovereignty over earth and heaven would not fix it. This means the problem is not situational — it is existential. It can only be resolved by a teaching that goes to the root of human suffering. The Gītā's necessity is established.

# 2.9
short: Sañjaya said: Having spoken thus to Hṛṣīkeśa (Kṛṣṇa), Arjuna, the scorcher of foes, said "I will not fight" to Govinda (Kṛṣṇa) and became silent.
long: The dramatic pause. After all his reasoning, Arjuna's conclusion is simple: "na yotsye" — I will not fight. Then silence. The Gītā's teaching begins from this silence, this refusal, this despair. Sañjaya notes Arjuna's two epithets: "Gudākeśa" (master of sleep, indicating alertness) and "Parantapa" (scorcher of foes) — both suggesting he is capable of fighting but choosing not to.

# 2.10
short: O Bhārata (Dhṛtarāṣṭra), to him who was grieving between the two armies, Hṛṣīkeśa, as if smiling, spoke these words.
long: "As if smiling" (prahasann iva) is crucial — Kṛṣṇa does not respond with alarm or frustration. The faint smile signals that he sees Arjuna's confusion from a higher vantage point, where the solution is already known. The teaching begins in an atmosphere of compassionate equanimity, not reactive urgency.

# 2.11
short: The Blessed Lord said: You grieve for those who should not be grieved for, and you speak words of wisdom. The wise grieve neither for the living nor for the dead.
long: Kṛṣṇa opens his teaching with a paradox: Arjuna thinks himself wise (uses words of wisdom) but is grieving in the wrong place. The Sāṅkhya teaching on the soul begins here — the wise (paṇḍitāḥ) do not grieve for the living or the dead, because they understand what the self really is. This verse introduces the entire metaphysical foundation of the Gītā.

# 2.12
short: Never was there a time when I did not exist, nor you, nor all these kings; nor in the future shall any of us cease to be.
long: The first direct statement of the soul's eternal nature. Kṛṣṇa speaks in the first person: "I, you, and all these kings" — past, present, future, all exist and will exist. The word "na" appears three times for emphasis. This verse refutes the fear of non-existence that drives Arjuna's grief: there is nothing to lose that can truly be lost.

# 2.13
short: Just as the soul in this body passes through childhood, youth, and old age, so also it passes into another body. The wise person is not deluded by this.
long: The soul's continuity through bodily change is compared to life's stages — everyone knows the body changes while the "I" persists. Death is just another such change. The wise person (dhīra) sees this and is not confused (na muhyati). This analogy is one of the Gītā's most accessible arguments for the soul's immortality.

# 2.14
short: The contact of the senses with objects — cold and heat, pleasure and pain — they come and go; they are impermanent. Bear them with endurance, O Arjuna.
long: The first practical instruction: sensory experiences are transient. The specific example of cold and heat (śītoṣṇa) is universal — every human knows these fluctuate. "Āgamāpāyinaḥ" — they come and they go. The instruction is not to eliminate sensation but to bear it (titikṣasva) — a key Vedāntic virtue. The ability to endure is itself a form of wisdom.

# 2.15
short: That person whom these (joys and sorrows) do not afflict, who is even-minded in pleasure and pain, the steadfast one — is fit for immortality.
long: The result of endurance: the one who is not shaken by pleasure or pain (sama-duḥkha-sukham) is called "mṛtyāmṛtatvāya kalpate" — fit for immortality. This is not stoic numbness but an inner steadiness. The Gītā's positive vision of liberation begins to emerge: it is about freedom from reactive suffering, not mere suppression of feeling.

# 2.16
short: The unreal has no existence and the real never ceases to be. The truth about both has been seen by the seers of truth.
long: The metaphysical foundation stated at its most compressed: "nāsato vidyate bhāvaḥ" — the unreal has no being; "nābhāvo vidyate sataḥ" — the real has no non-being. This is the Sat-Asat distinction. The body is asat (changing, perishable); the Self is sat (unchanging, real). The ṛṣis who "saw" this (dṛṣṭavantaḥ) received it in direct insight, not inference.

# 2.17
short: Know that by which all this is pervaded to be indestructible. No one is able to cause the destruction of that imperishable one.
long: Having stated the sat/asat distinction, Kṛṣṇa now identifies the real: that which pervades everything (yena sarvam idaṃ tatam). It is avināśi (indestructible) — no one can destroy it. This is the first direct description of the Self (Ātman/Brahman) as all-pervading. The word "tatam" (spread out) will recur in ch.9 describing the Lord pervading the cosmos.

# 2.18
short: These bodies of the embodied, eternal, indestructible, and immeasurable Self are said to have an end. Therefore fight, O Arjuna.
long: The first direct call to action based on the metaphysics just stated. The bodies perish; the Self within them does not. The logic is: grieve if the Self died — but it doesn't. Only the body ends, and the body is finite by definition (anāśinaḥ = indestructible — referring to the Self; the bodies themselves are antavanta = having an end). "Therefore fight" follows necessarily.

# 2.19
short: He who thinks this (Self) is a slayer and he who thinks this is slain — both of them fail to perceive the truth. This neither slays nor is slain.
long: The Self is neither agent nor patient of killing. The error is categorical: identifying the self with the body. If the body is slain, the Self is untouched; if the warrior strikes down a body, the Self within remains. "Na hanyate" — it is not killed — is the positive statement that will become famous.

Six negatives: not born, not slain, not coming into being, not perishing, not killed when the body is killed. This verse will later be quoted across all Vedāntic commentary traditions.

# 2.21
short: O Arjuna, he who knows this Self to be indestructible, eternal, unborn, and imperishable — how can such a person slay anyone, or cause anyone to be slain?
long: A logical argument: given the Self's nature (avināśi, nitya, aja, avyaya), the very concept of slaying becomes incoherent. If the Self cannot be destroyed, who is being destroyed? The question is rhetorical — the answer reveals that the warrior who understands the Self can engage in battle without the moral stain of "killing."

# 2.22
short: Just as a person puts on new garments, giving up old ones, similarly the soul accepts new material bodies, giving up the old and useless ones.
long: The most famous analogy in the Gītā for rebirth: changing clothes. The soul (dehī) is the wearer; bodies are garments. "Vihāya jarāṃsi" — casting off worn-out ones. The analogy is elegant and immediate — every listener has changed clothes and knows the wearer remains unchanged. Death is thus a wardrobe change, not annihilation.

# 2.23
short: Weapons do not cut this Self, fire does not burn it, water does not wet it, and wind does not dry it.
long: Four great physical forces — sword, fire, water, wind — are each negated. This is not mere poetry but a systematic refutation of every mode of physical destruction. The Self has no material property that can be acted upon by material agents. The verse operates like an ancient chemistry: each element tried, each failing. The Self is beyond the elements.
