# 16.1
short: The Lord said — Fearlessness, purity of being, steadfastness in knowledge-yoga, generosity, self-control, sacrifice, study of scripture, austerity, uprightness —
long: Chapter 16 distinguishes the divine (daivī) from the demonic (āsurī) qualities in human beings. The daivī sampad (divine endowment) is a 26-quality list (16.1-3) representing what the sattvic, spiritually mature person cultivates. First group: abhayam (fearlessness — the foundation of spiritual life), sattva-saṃśuddhi (purity of being — sattva in one's very constitution), jñāna-yoga-vyavasthiti (steadfastness in jñāna-yoga), dāna (generosity), dama (self-control of senses), yajña (sacrifice/ritual), svādhyāya (scripture study), tapas (austerity), ārjava (uprightness).
wbw:
  abhayaṃ sattva-saṃśuddhiḥ | phrase | fearlessness, purity of being
  jñāna-yoga-vyavasthitiḥ | phrase | steadfastness in knowledge-yoga
  dānaṃ damaś ca yajñaś ca | phrase | generosity, self-control, and sacrifice
  svādhyāyas tapa ārjavam | phrase | scripture study, austerity, uprightness
---
# 16.2
short: Non-harming, truth, freedom from anger, renunciation, tranquility, non-slandering, compassion for beings, freedom from covetousness, gentleness, modesty, absence of fickleness —
long: Continuing the list: ahiṃsā (non-harming), satya (truth), akrodha (freedom from anger), tyāga (renunciation), śānti (tranquility/peace), apaiśuna (absence of fault-finding/slander — literally not-making-others-small), bhūteṣu dayā (compassion for beings), aloluptva (freedom from covetousness), mārdava (gentleness/mildness), hrī (modesty/shame at wrongdoing), acāpala (absence of fickleness/inconstancy). Each of these is a specific virtue that emerges from sattva and enables spiritual growth.

# 16.3
short: Vigor, patience, fortitude, purity, freedom from ill will, absence of excessive pride — these belong to one born to divine destiny, O Bhārata.
long: "Tejaḥ kṣamā dhṛtiḥ śaucam adroho nātimanitā / bhavanti sampadaṃ daivīm abhijātasya bhārata." Completing the list: tejas (vigor/energy/brilliance), kṣamā (patience/forgiveness), dhṛti (fortitude/steadiness), śauca (purity), adroha (freedom from ill will/hostility), nātimanitā (absence of excessive pride — note it says excessive; some self-respect is appropriate). "Sampadaṃ daivīm abhijātasya" — belonging to one born to the divine endowment (daivī sampad). These are the 26 divine qualities complete.

# 16.4
short: Hypocrisy, arrogance, conceit, anger, harshness, and ignorance — these belong to one born to demonic destiny, O Pārtha.
long: "Dambho darpo'timānaś ca krodhaḥ pāruṣyam eva ca / ajñānaṃ cābhijātasya pārtha sampadaṃ āsurīm." The āsurī sampad (demonic endowment) in six qualities: dambha (hypocrisy — performing righteousness for show), darpa (arrogance — pride without foundation), atimāna (conceit — excessive pride), krodha (anger), pāruṣya (harshness/cruelty — in speech and action), ajñāna (ignorance — the root that underlies all the others). These are the āsurī traits. Notably the list of demonic qualities is much shorter than the divine — six versus twenty-six.

# 16.5
short: Divine destiny leads to liberation; demonic destiny leads to bondage. Do not grieve — you are born to divine destiny, O Pāṇḍava.
long: "Daivī sampad vimokṣāya nibandhāyāsurī matā / mā śucaḥ sampadaṃ daivīm abhijāto'si pāṇḍava." Daivī sampad → vimokṣāya (leads to liberation). Āsurī sampad → nibandhāya (leads to bondage). Then Krishna adds a personal word: "mā śucaḥ" — do not grieve. "Sampadaṃ daivīm abhijāto'si pāṇḍava" — you are born to divine endowment, O Pāṇḍava. The teaching isn't just philosophy — it's addressed to Arjuna personally. He needs to hear: you are on the right side of this distinction. Don't worry.

# 16.6
short: There are two types of created beings in this world — the divine and the demonic. The divine has been described at length; hear from Me about the demonic, O Pārtha.
long: "Dvau bhūta-sargau loke'smin daiva āsura eva ca / daivo vistaraśaḥ prokta āsuraṃ pārtha me śṛṇu." Two fundamental types of created beings in this world — daiva (divine) and āsura (demonic). The daivī has been described at length (vistaraśaḥ) — the twenty-six qualities. Now hear about the āsurī. This binary is not meant to permanently categorize people — it describes tendencies, and tendencies can shift. The chapter is not about condemning specific people but about recognizing and transforming qualities within oneself.

# 16.7
short: The demonic people know neither what should be done nor what should not be done. Neither purity, nor right conduct, nor truth is found in them.
long: "Pravṛttiṃ ca nivṛttiṃ ca janā na vidur āsurāḥ / na śaucaṃ nāpi cācāro na satyaṃ teṣu vidyate." The āsurī person doesn't know pravṛtti (what to do — right action) or nivṛtti (what to refrain from — right restraint). This is the epistemological failure at the root of the demonic character: not knowing what is dharma. From this ignorance flows: no śauca (purity), no ācāra (right conduct/behavior), no satya (truth). The three pillars of a life aligned with dharma are absent.

# 16.8
short: They say — the world is without truth, without a foundation, without a God — not produced by any causal sequence. What else — only desire is the cause.
long: "Asatyam apratiṣṭhaṃ te jagad āhur anīśvaram / aparaspara-sambhūtaṃ kim anyat kāma-haitukam." The āsurī worldview: "asatyam" — the world is without truth (no dharma, no moral order). "Apratiṣṭham" — without foundation/grounding. "Anīśvaram" — without a God/Lord (no divine principle). "Aparaspara-sambhūtam" — not produced by any mutual causal sequence (no meaningful causation). "Kim anyat kāma-haitukam" — what else — only desire (kāma) is the cause. This is nihilism: the universe is purposeless, driven only by desire, with no order, truth, or divinity.

long: "Yaḥ śāstra-vidhim utsṛjya vartate kāma-kārataḥ / na sa siddhim avāpnoti na sukhaṃ na parāṃ gatim." "Śāstra-vidhim utsṛjya" — abandoning the injunctions of scripture (śāstra-vidhi — the instructions/ordinances of the shastra). "Vartate kāma-kārataḥ" — acts solely by desire (kāma-kāra — desire as the acting principle). "Na sa siddhim avāpnoti" — that person does not attain siddhi (perfection). "Na sukham" — not happiness. "Na parāṃ gatim" — not the Supreme Goal. Three things lost when one abandons śāstra: perfection, happiness, liberation.

# 16.24
short: Therefore, let scripture be your authority in determining what should be done and what should not be done. Having known the scriptural injunction, you should act here.
long: "Tasmāc chāstraṃ pramāṇaṃ te kāryākārya-vyavasthitau / jñātvā śāstra-vidhānoktaṃ karma kartum ihārhasi." "Tasmāc chāstraṃ pramāṇaṃ te" — therefore, let scripture be your authority (pramāṇa — the valid means of knowledge). "Kāryākārya-vyavasthitau" — in determining what should be done (kārya) and what should not be done (akārya). "Jñātvā śāstra-vidhānoktaṃ karma kartum ihārhasi" — having known what the scriptural instruction prescribes, you should perform action here. This is the conclusion of chapter 16: scripture as the guide to dharmic action. But "scripture" must be understood as living wisdom, not dead rule.
