# 8.1
short: Arjuna asks — What is Brahman? What is Adhyātma? What is karma? What is called Adhibhūta? And what is said to be Adhidaiva?
long: Arjuna picks up the three terms from 7.30 — asking about each. Five questions in one verse: Brahman (the absolute), Adhyātma (the inner self), karma (action/cause), Adhibhūta (the divine in matter/beings), Adhidaiva (the divine in the cosmic realm). These are the great metaphysical categories of existence. Chapter 8 answers these systematically, then turns to the most practical question: what should one think of at death?

# 8.2
short: Who is Adhiyajña here in this body, and how? And at the time of death, how are You to be known by those of steadied selves?
long: Completing Arjuna's questions: Adhiyajña (the divine in sacrifice) — who is it in the body, and how? Then the question most personal and urgent: "prayāṇa-kāle ca kathaṃ jñeyo'si niyatātmabhiḥ" — at the time of departure (death), how are You to be known by those of steadied selves? This is the real question behind all the metaphysics: does any of this matter at death? Krishna's answer will dominate the chapter.

# 8.3
short: The Lord says — The Imperishable is Brahman; the individual self is called Adhyātma; the creative force that causes the arising of beings is called karma.
long: Krishna's answers: Brahman = akṣara (the imperishable, the immutable absolute). Adhyātma = svabhāva — the individual's own nature, the principle of selfhood in the individual. Karma = "visarga" — the creative outpouring, the force that brings forth being from the unmanifest. This is karma in its cosmic sense — not just individual action but the primordial creative energy that makes existence continuously arise. The background to individual karma is universal creative impulsion.

  visargaḥ | noun | the outpouring/creation
  karma-saṃjñitaḥ | adjective | is called karma

# 8.4
short: Adhibhūta is the perishable realm; Adhidaiva is the cosmic Person (Puruṣa); and I Myself am Adhiyajña here in the body, O best of embodied ones.
long: Completing the answers: Adhibhūta = kṣara bhāva (the perishable/changing realm — all that comes and goes in the manifest world). Adhidaiva = Puruṣa (the cosmic Person — the divine intelligence presiding over the senses and cosmic order). Adhiyajña = aham (I myself) am Adhiyajña in this body. How? Through yajna — every act of sacrifice, every breath of reciprocal exchange — the divine is present. The sacrificial principle in the body is Krishna Himself.

# 8.5
short: Whoever, at the time of death, leaves the body while remembering Me alone — comes to My state. Of this there is no doubt.
long: The central claim of chapter 8: "anta-kāle ca mām eva smaran muktvā kalevaram / yaḥ prayāti sa mad-bhāvaṃ yāti nāsty atra saṃśayaḥ." At death, remembering Me alone — attains My state. "Mad-bhāva" — My being, My nature. Why "at death" specifically? Because death is the moment of ultimate concentration — every habit of mind, every dominant tendency, rises to the surface. The last thought is the summation of a life. Hence the urgency throughout the Gita of training the mind now.

# 8.6
short: Whatever state of being one remembers at the time of leaving the body, to that state alone one goes, O Kaunteya — always transformed by that state.
long: The universal law stated: "yaṃ yaṃ vāpi smaran bhāvaṃ tyajaty ante kalevaram / taṃ tam evaiti kaunteya sadā tad-bhāva-bhāvitaḥ." Whatever state of being one remembers at death — whatever object, whatever principle, whatever person — one goes to that state. "Sadā tad-bhāva-bhāvitaḥ" — always shaped by that state. A lifetime of thinking and loving shapes the dying mind. Thought at death is not arbitrary — it is the distillation of habitual consciousness.

# 8.7
short: Therefore, at all times remember Me and fight. With mind and intellect fixed on Me, you will come to Me without doubt.
long: The practical conclusion of 8.5-6: since the death-thought determines the destination, the practice is to saturate consciousness with Krishna now. "Tasmāt sarveṣu kāleṣu mām anusmara yudhya ca" — therefore at all times remember Me, and fight (do your duty). The practice is not withdrawal from life but living while maintaining divine remembrance. "Mayyarpita-mano-buddhiḥ" — with mind and intellect fixed in Me. Then: "mām evaika-dhiyo yanti" — no doubt, they come to Me.

# 8.8
short: With the mind trained by practice, not straying to anything else, one who meditates on the Supreme Divine Person goes to Him, O Pārtha.
long: The method: "abhyāsa-yoga-yuktena cetasā nānya-gāminā" — with the mind made firm through the yoga of practice (abhyāsa), not going elsewhere. "Paramaṃ puruṣaṃ divyaṃ yāti pārthānucintayan" — meditating on the Supreme Divine Person, one goes to Him. "Abhyāsa" — repeated practice — is the key. The mind by nature wanders; practice channels it. "Nānya-gāminā" — not going elsewhere, not distracted by other objects. Single-pointed contemplation of the divine.

Each attribute points to a different facet of the infinite. Together they describe what is beyond description.

# 8.10
short: At the time of death, with unwavering mind, devoted with love and the power of yoga — fixing the prāṇa between the eyebrows — such a one reaches the Supreme Divine Person.
long: (Concluding 8.9) "Prayāṇa-kāle manasācalena bhaktyā yukto yoga-balena caiva / bhruvōr madhye prāṇam āveśya samyak" — at the time of death, with unwavering mind, with devotion and the power of yoga, directing the prāṇa (life-force) perfectly between the eyebrows. "Sa taṃ paraṃ puruṣam upaiti divyam" — one reaches that Supreme Divine Person. The āñjā (brow) center is the traditional exit-point in yogic death practice. The whole discipline of life leads to this moment of conscious departure.

# 8.11

"Yad icchanto brahmacaryaṃ caranti" — desiring which they practice celibacy (brahmacaryam — conservation of energy for the divine). This is the highest goal — the akṣara. Krishna will now give the method for reaching it: the yoga of controlled departure at death.

# 8.12
short: Closing all the gates (senses), confining the mind in the heart, drawing the prāṇa to the head — established in yoga-concentration.
long: (Continues in 8.13) The yoga of conscious dying. "Sarva-dvārāṇi saṃyamya" — closing all gates (the sense organs). "Mano hṛdi nirudhya ca" — confining the mind in the heart. "Mūrdhny ādhāyātmanaḥ prāṇam" — drawing the life-breath (prāṇa) to the crown of the head. "Āsthito yoga-dhāraṇām" — established in yogic concentration. This is systematic withdrawal of consciousness from the periphery to the center, then upward — the reversal of incarnation. What comes in at birth goes out at death by the same paths, consciously controlled.

# 8.13
short: Uttering the single-syllable OM — Brahman — remembering Me, one who departs leaving the body reaches the supreme goal.
long: The culminating act: "om ity ekākṣaraṃ brahma vyāharan mām anusmaran / yaḥ prayāti tyajan dehaṃ sa yāti paramāṃ gatim." OM — the one-syllable Brahman — is uttered. Simultaneously: "mām anusmaran" — remembering Me (Krishna). The union of the primal sound (OM) and devotional remembrance (bhakti). The departure: "tyajan deham" — leaving the body. The destination: "paramāṃ gatim" — the supreme goal. OM is not merely a sound but the vibration of the Absolute itself — the fitting vehicle for final departure.

# 8.14
short: I am easily attained by the yogi who thinks of Me constantly, with no other thought, O Pārtha — one who is always disciplined.
long: The simplest statement of the chapter's message: "ananya-cetāḥ satataṃ yo māṃ smarati nityaśaḥ" — whoever, with undivided mind, constantly remembers Me without break. "Tasyāhaṃ sulabhaḥ pārtha nitya-yuktasya yoginaḥ" — to that yogi, always disciplined, I am "sulabha" — easily attained. Not difficult — easy. The qualification is totality: "ananya-cetāḥ" (no other thought). The barrier is not effort but divided attention. Undivided love finds the divine immediately.

# 8.15
short: Having reached Me, the great souls do not undergo rebirth — that unstable, sorrowful abode — for they have attained the highest perfection.
long: "Mām upetya punar janma duḥkhālayam aśāśvatam / nāpnuvanti mahātmānaḥ saṃsiddhiṃ paramāṃ gatāḥ." Having reached Me (mām upetya), the great souls do not return to rebirth. What is rebirth? "Duḥkhālayam aśāśvatam" — the abode of suffering, the unstable home. Impermanence is suffering — not a metaphysical assertion but a practical one: everything conditioned is uncertain, temporary, insufficient. Those who have attained the highest (paramāṃ saṃsiddhim) are done with this cycle.

# 8.16
short: From the realm of Brahmā downward, all worlds are subject to return, O Arjuna. But reaching Me, O Kaunteya, there is no rebirth.
long: The hierarchical view of cosmic existence: all worlds — from the lowest to the highest (Brahmaloka — the realm of Brahmā, the creator himself) — are impermanent. Even the highest heavens are temporary. "Ābrahma-bhuvanāl lokāḥ punar āvartino'rjuna" — from Brahma-loka downward, all undergo return. Only reaching Krishna — the absolute — is permanent. This prevents spiritual materialism: striving for higher worlds still leaves one in saṃsāra. Only the absolute ends the cycle.

# 8.17
short: Those who know that the day of Brahmā lasts a thousand yugas and his night lasts a thousand yugas — they know day and night.
long: The cosmic time-scale: Brahmā's day = 1000 mahā-yugas (4.32 billion years). Brahmā's night = equal duration. One "day" of the creator spans geological ages. What is the point? Even Brahmā's creation is within time. The world that emerges at Brahmā's dawn dissolves at his dusk — and is recreated at the next dawn. This vast scale relativizes human existence: not to minimize it but to show that even the grandest cosmic cycles are within time, hence impermanent, hence not the ultimate.

# 8.18
short: From the Unmanifest, all beings come forth at Brahmā's dawn; at Brahmā's dusk they dissolve back into what is called the Unmanifest.
long: "Avyaktād vyaktayaḥ sarvāḥ prabhavanty ahar-āgame / rātry-āgame pralīyante tatraivāvyakta-saṃjñake." At the dawn of Brahmā's day, all beings emerge from the unmanifest (avyakta); at dusk they dissolve back into what is called the unmanifest. This is the rhythm of cosmic manifestation: breath in, breath out. Creation is not a one-time event but a continuous rhythmic process on a vast scale. Everything in the manifest world participates in this cycle.

# 8.19
short: This same multitude of beings, arising again and again, dissolves at the approach of night — and arises again at dawn, O Pārtha, helplessly.
long: "Bhūta-grāmaḥ sa evāyaṃ bhūtvā bhūtvā pralīyate / rātry-āgame'vaśaḥ pārtha prabhavaty ahar-āgame." The same multitude of beings — "bhūtvā bhūtvā" (arising again and again) — helplessly (avaśaḥ) dissolves at night, arises at dawn. "Avaśaḥ" — without choice, helplessly — is the key word. This is the condition of existence that is not directed by the divine: involuntary cycling. The path out of this helplessness is the conscious orientation toward the imperishable that the chapter describes.

# 8.20
short: But beyond this unmanifest, there is another unmanifest — eternal, which does not dissolve when all beings dissolve.
long: "Paras tasmāt tu bhāvo'nyo'vyakto'vyaktāt sanātanaḥ / yaḥ sa sarveṣu bhūteṣu naśyatsu na vinaśyati." Beyond the cosmic unmanifest (avyakta — 8.18, the source/sink of cyclic creation), there is another (anya) — the sanātana avyakta, the eternal unmanifest. When all beings dissolve — "naśyatsu" — this other does not dissolve. Two different unmanifests: the cyclic one (source of temporal creation) and the eternal one (beyond all cycles). The eternal is Krishna's supreme nature.

# 8.22

# 8.23

Some read this literally (cosmic routes). Others read it symbolically (the state of consciousness at death). Either way, the message is: orientation toward the eternal matters.

# 8.24
short: Fire, light, day, the bright half of the month, the six months of the northern course of the sun — departing by this path, those who know Brahman reach Brahman.
long: The bright path (devayāna): fire (agni), light (jyoti), day (ahaḥ), bright fortnight (śukla-pakṣa), six northern months (uttarāyaṇa). "Tatra prayātā gacchanti brahma brahma-vido janāḥ" — those who know Brahman, departing by this path, go to Brahman. Symbolically: fire = luminous consciousness; light = clarity; day = awakened state; bright fortnight = waxing energy; northern course = upward trajectory. Those departing in this state of inner light reach the light.

# 8.25
short: Smoke, night, the dark fortnight, the six months of the southern course of the sun — the yogi obtains the lunar light (heavens) and returns.
long: The dark path (pitṛyāna): smoke (dhūma), night (rātriḥ), dark fortnight (kṛṣṇa-pakṣa), six southern months (dakṣiṇāyana). "Tatra cāndramasaṃ jyotiḥ yogī prāpya nivartate" — the yogi obtaining the lunar light (heavens) returns. This is temporary elevation — the pitṛ-loka, the ancestors' realm, is pleasant but not permanent. Karmic merit is enjoyed, then exhausted, and rebirth follows. The heavens are a waiting room, not a destination. Return to earth is built in.

# 8.26
short: These two paths — bright and dark — are considered eternal in the world. By one a person does not return; by the other one returns again.
long: "Śukla-kṛṣṇe gatī hy ete jagataḥ śāśvate mate / ekayā yāty anāvṛttim anyayekayā" — the two paths (bright and dark) are considered eternal in the world. One leads to non-return; the other leads to return. These represent two fundamental orientations: toward the eternal (leading out of saṃsāra) and toward the temporal (leading back into it). # 8.27
short: O Pārtha, knowing these two paths, the yogi is not deluded. Therefore, at all times be established in yoga.
long: "Naite sṛtī pārtha jānan yogī muhyati kaścana / tasmāt sarveṣu kāleṣu yoga-yukto bhavārjuna." Knowing these two paths, no yogi is deluded. The knowledge protects. "Tasmāt sarveṣu kāleṣu yoga-yukto bhavārjuna" — therefore, at all times, be established in yoga. The cosmic teaching resolves into a personal imperative: the grand map of death-paths leads to the simple practice of being yoga-yukta (disciplined in yoga) now, in this life, at every moment.

# 8.28
short: The yogi, knowing all this, surpasses whatever meritorious fruits are taught in the Vedas, in sacrifices, in austerities, and in charities — and reaches the supreme, primeval abode.
long: "Vedeṣu yajñeṣu tapaḥsu caiva dāneṣu yat puṇya-phalaṃ pradiṣṭam / atyeti tat sarvam idaṃ viditvā yogī paraṃ sthānam upaiti cādyam." The yogi who knows all this (chapters 7-8 culminating here) surpasses all the meritorious results prescribed in the Vedas, sacrifices, austerities, and charities. Not by performing them more — by transcending the entire framework of merit-seeking. The yogi reaches "paraṃ sthānam ādyam" — the supreme, primordial abode. This is the fruition: not accumulating religious merit but transcending the merit-system entirely through yoga and knowledge.
