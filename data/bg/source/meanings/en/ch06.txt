# 6.1
short: The Blessed Lord said: One who performs the required duty without depending on the fruit of action is a sannyāsī (renunciant) and a yogī — not one who is without fire and without action.
long: Chapter 6 defines yoga on its own terms. Kṛṣṇa dismisses two easy misidentifications: the sannyāsī is not the one who has given up sacred fires (anagni), and the yogī is not the one who has given up all action (akriya). Both positive qualities — engagement in duty + non-dependence on fruit — are required. The inner stance defines the sannyāsī; not external appearances.

# 6.2
short: Know that what they call renunciation (sannyāsa) is yoga. No one becomes a yogī without renouncing desire, O Pāṇḍava.
long: The equation: sannyāsa = yoga. They are not two separate paths but the same goal described differently. The defining condition: "na hy asannyasta-saṅkalpaḥ yogī bhavati kaścana" — no one becomes a yogī who has not renounced saṅkalpa (desire, intentional grasping for results). Sannyāsa, properly understood, is the inner renunciation of result-seeking that karma yoga requires.

# 6.3
short: For the sage who wishes to ascend yoga, action is said to be the cause. For that same sage who has ascended yoga, stillness (śama) is said to be the cause.
long: Two stages in yoga practice: the ascent and the summit. For the one climbing (ārurukṣu), karma (action) is the means. For the one who has climbed (yogā-rūḍha), śama (quietude/stillness) is the means. This verse introduces a natural spiritual progression: active engagement gives way to increasing interior stillness as the yogi matures. Both stages are valid and necessary.

# 6.4

# 6.5
short: Let one lift oneself by the self — do not let the self be lowered. For the self alone is the friend of the self, and the self alone is the enemy of the self.
long: One of the Gītā's most celebrated verses: the call to self-reliance. "Uddhared ātmanā ātmānaṃ" — lift yourself by yourself. The self can be both the instrument of liberation and the obstacle. "Ātmā bandhuḥ ātmā ripuḥ" — the self is its own friend and its own enemy. This is not abstract — it refers to the higher self (discriminating intelligence) lifting the lower self (ego/habit).

# 6.6
short: The self is the friend of the self for one who has conquered the self by the self. For one who has not conquered the self, the self acts as an enemy, like an external foe.
long: The conditional: for the "jitātmā" (self-conquered), the self is a friend. For the one who has not conquered the self, the self acts as an external enemy — "vairi iva" — like a foe. The metaphor is precise: an unconquered self is not merely neutral; it actively works against one's liberation, leading toward destructive patterns, attachments, and delusion.

# 6.7
short: For the self-conquered, whose mind is pacified, the highest self is steadily in a state of equanimity — in cold and heat, in pleasure and pain, in honor and dishonor.
long: The self-conquered (jitātmā) whose mind is śānta (pacified) has the paramātmā (the supreme self) steadily within them — or better: they are steadily established in equanimity across all pairs of opposites: cold/heat (śītoṣṇa), pleasure/pain (sukha-duḥkha), honor/dishonor (māna-apamāna). The outer world no longer has the power to destabilize the inner ground.

# 6.8
short: One who is satisfied with knowledge and direct experience (vijñāna), who is stable, who has conquered the senses, who regards alike a lump of earth, a stone, and gold — that one is said to be a yogi who is "yoked" (yukta).
long: Portrait of the yukta (yoked/enlightened) yogi: jñāna-vijñāna-tṛptātmā (satisfied with knowledge and direct experiential wisdom), kūṭa-sthā (stable, like an anvil), vijita-indriya (senses conquered), and sama-loṣṭāśma-kañcana (regarding earth-clod, stone, and gold with equal eye). The last quality (equanimity toward wealth) is a concrete, testable marker of genuine inner freedom.

# 6.9
short: One who is equal toward well-wishers, friends, enemies, the neutral, the mediators, the hateful, the relatives, the virtuous, and the sinful — that person excels.

All nine types receive the same inner regard. Not because differences are denied — but because the Self in each is the same. "Sa viśiṣyate" — that person excels/is distinguished.

# 6.16
short: Yoga is not for one who eats too much or too little, nor for one who sleeps too much or keeps too little, O Arjuna.
long: The yoga of the middle way: extremes of āhāra (food — eating too much or too little) and svapna (sleep — sleeping too much or too little) both prevent yoga. This is practically important: the body must be neither starved nor over-nourished, neither exhausted nor over-rested. The yogi's practice rests on a well-maintained physical foundation — not on torturing or indulging the body.

# 6.18

# 6.26
short: Wherever the wavering, unsteady mind wanders away — restrain it from there and bring it back again, under the control of the Self alone.
