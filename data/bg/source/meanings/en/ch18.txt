# 18.1
short: Arjuna said — O mighty-armed, I wish to know separately the truth of sannyāsa and of tyāga, O Hṛṣīkeśa, O Keśiniṣūdana.
long: The final chapter opens with Arjuna asking for the distinction between two terms that have appeared throughout: sannyāsa (renunciation — typically of action itself) and tyāga (relinquishment — of the fruits of action). These two Sanskrit words are easy to conflate but signify different practices. The Gītā's answer will clarify: tyāga of fruits while performing action is the true teaching; outright abandonment of all action (sannyāsa) is typically impractical. This sets up the chapter's major review of all prior teachings, culminating in the Gītā's ultimate message.
wbw:
  sannyāsasya mahābāho | phrase | of sannyāsa, O mighty-armed
  tattvam icchāmi veditum | phrase | I wish to know the truth
  tyāgasya ca hṛṣīkeśa | phrase | and of tyāga, O Hṛṣīkeśa
  pṛthak keśiniṣūdana | phrase | separately, O Keśiniṣūdana
---
# 18.2
short: The Lord said — Sages understand sannyāsa as the giving up of desire-motivated actions; the wise declare the relinquishment of all action's fruits to be tyāga.
long: "Kāmyānāṃ karmaṇāṃ nyāsaṃ sannyāsaṃ kavayo viduḥ / sarva-karma-phala-tyāgaṃ prāhus tyāgaṃ vicakṣaṇāḥ." Sannyāsa (per the sages): "kāmyānāṃ karmaṇāṃ nyāsam" — the giving up (nyāsa) of kāmya-karmas (actions motivated by desire). Tyāga (per the wise): "sarva-karma-phala-tyāgam" — relinquishment (tyāga) of the fruits (phala) of all actions. This distinction is important: sannyāsa means not performing desire-driven actions; tyāga means performing all actions while releasing attachment to their results. The latter is the Gītā's teaching from chapters 2-3.

  sannyāsaṃ kavayo viduḥ | phrase | sages understand as sannyāsa
  sarva-karma-phala-tyāgam | phrase | the relinquishment of all action's fruits
  prāhus tyāgaṃ vicakṣaṇāḥ | phrase | the wise declare to be tyāga

# 18.3
short: "Action should be abandoned as evil," say some thinkers. "Sacrifice, giving, and austerity are not to be abandoned," say others.
long: "Tyājyaṃ doṣavad ity eke karma prāhur manīṣiṇaḥ / yajña-dāna-tapaḥ-karma na tyājyam iti cāpare." Two opposing views among thinkers: (1) "Tyājyaṃ doṣavad ity eke" — "action should be abandoned as flawed" — these are those who advocate total renunciation (action as inherently binding). (2) "Yajña-dāna-tapaḥ-karma na tyājyam" — "sacrifice, giving, and austerity should not be abandoned" — those who insist certain obligatory actions must be maintained. Krishna will give his own resolution to this debate in the following verses.

# 18.4
short: Hear My conclusion about tyāga, O best of Bhāratas — for tyāga has been declared threefold, O tiger among men.
long: "Niścayaṃ śṛṇu me tatra tyāge bharata-sattama / tyāgo hi puruṣa-vyāghra tri-vidhaḥ samprakīrtitaḥ." "Niścayaṃ śṛṇu me" — hear My conclusion/determination. "Tatra tyāge" — on the matter of tyāga. "Tri-vidhaḥ samprakīrtitaḥ" — tyāga has been declared threefold. As with food (17.8-10), sacrifice (17.11-13), austerity (17.14-19), and giving (17.20-22), tyāga too is analyzed through the three guṇas. Krishna's position will be: sattvic tyāga (fruit-renunciation while performing action) is the right answer, and outright abandonment of obligatory action is tamasic.

# 18.5
short: Acts of sacrifice, giving, and austerity are not to be abandoned — they should be performed. Sacrifice, giving, and austerity purify the wise.
long: "Yajña-dāna-tapaḥ-karma na tyājyaṃ kāryam eva tat / yajño dānaṃ tapaś caiva pāvanāni manīṣiṇām." "Yajña-dāna-tapaḥ-karma na tyājyam" — sacrifice, giving, and austerity are not to be abandoned. "Kāryam eva tat" — they must be performed (kārya — what is to be done). "Yajño dānaṃ tapaś caiva pāvanāni manīṣiṇām" — sacrifice, giving, and austerity are purifiers (pāvanāni) of the wise. These three are not optional religious extras — they are the karmas that purify consciousness. Abandoning them (in the name of "renunciation") is the wrong understanding of tyāga.

# 18.6
short: Even these actions should be performed, abandoning attachment and fruits. That is My final and definite view, O Pārtha.
long: "Etāny api tu karmāṇi saṅgaṃ tyaktvā phalāni ca / kartavyānīti me pārtha niścitaṃ matam uttamam." "Etāny api tu karmāṇi" — even these actions (sacrifice, giving, austerity). "Saṅgaṃ tyaktvā phalāni ca" — abandoning saṅga (attachment) and phalāni (fruits). "Kartavyāni" — must be performed (kartavya — what is to be done, duty). "Iti me pārtha niścitaṃ matam uttamam" — this is My settled (niścitam), highest (uttamam) opinion. The formula is the heart of karma-yoga: perform all obligatory action, release attachment and outcomes, keep performing.

# 18.7
short: The giving up of a prescribed action is not proper. Its abandonment through delusion is declared to be tamasic.
long: "Niyatasya tu sannyāsaḥ karmaṇo nopapadyate / mohāt tasya parityāgas tāmasaḥ parikīrtitaḥ." "Niyatasya tu sannyāsaḥ karmaṇaḥ" — the giving up of niyata (prescribed, obligatory) action. "Nopapadyate" — is not proper/is not acceptable. "Mohāt tasya parityāgaḥ" — its abandonment through moha (delusion). "Tāmasaḥ parikīrtitaḥ" — declared to be tamasic. The delusion referred to: believing that obligatory action is inherently bad, or that complete inaction is spiritually purer than right action. Tamasic tyāga = abandoning duty from confusion about what is truly "renunciation."

# 18.8
short: "This action is painful, this is bodily distress" — one who abandons action from this reasoning performs rajasic tyāga. He does not gain the fruit of tyāga.
long: "Duḥkham ity eva yat karma kāya-kleśa-bhayāt tyajet / sa kṛtvā rājasaṃ tyāgaṃ naiva tyāga-phalaṃ labhet." "Duḥkham ity eva yat karma" — whatever action is thought to be painful. "Kāya-kleśa-bhayāt tyajet" — abandoned from fear of bodily distress (kāya-kleśa). This is rajasic tyāga: abandoning action not from wisdom but from discomfort — "this is too hard, too painful, too inconvenient." "Sa kṛtvā rājasaṃ tyāgam" — having performed rajasic tyāga. "Naiva tyāga-phalaṃ labhet" — does not gain the fruit of tyāga. Avoiding effort is not renunciation — it's just avoidance.

# 18.9
short: When prescribed action is performed — thinking "this must be done" — abandoning attachment and fruit — that tyāga is held to be sattvic.
long: "Kāryam ity eva yat karma niyataṃ kriyate'rjuna / saṅgaṃ tyaktvā phalaṃ caiva sa tyāgaḥ sāttviko mataḥ." "Kāryam ity eva yat karma" — whatever action is performed with the sense "this must be done." "Niyataṃ kriyate" — prescribed action performed. "Saṅgaṃ tyaktvā phalaṃ caiva" — abandoning attachment and fruit. "Sa tyāgaḥ sāttviko mataḥ" — that tyāga is held as sattvic. This is the resolution: sattvic tyāga is not abandonment of action — it is action performed dutifully with the abandonment of attachment and results. This is the Gītā's karma-yoga distilled.

# 18.10
short: One of tyāga who is sattvic, who has no doubt — neither hates unpleasant action nor is attached to pleasant action.
long: "Na dveṣṭy akuśalaṃ karma kuśale nānuṣajjate / tyāgī sattva-samāviṣṭo medhāvī chinna-saṃśayaḥ." "Na dveṣṭy akuśalaṃ karma" — does not hate unpleasant/difficult action. "Kuśale nānuṣajjate" — nor becomes attached to pleasant/skillful action. "Tyāgī sattva-samāviṣṭaḥ" — the tyāgī (one who has truly renounced) who is pervaded by sattva. "Medhāvī chinna-saṃśayaḥ" — intelligent, with doubts cut away. The sattvic tyāgī has equanimity toward action itself — not drawn to agreeable tasks or repelled by disagreeable ones. Action is performed based on what is right, not what is comfortable.

# 18.11
short: For an embodied being, it is not possible to completely abandon action. But one who abandons the fruit of action — that one is called a tyāgī.
long: "Na hi deha-bhṛtā śakyaṃ tyaktuṃ karmāṇy aśeṣataḥ / yas tu karma-phala-tyāgī sa tyāgīty abhidhīyate." "Na hi deha-bhṛtā śakyaṃ tyaktuṃ karmāṇy aśeṣataḥ" — for an embodied being (deha-bhṛt — one who bears a body), it is not possible to abandon all action completely (aśeṣataḥ — without remainder). As long as one has a body, one acts — breathing, eating, thinking are all action. "Yas tu karma-phala-tyāgī" — but one who abandons the fruits of action. "Sa tyāgīty abhidhīyate" — that one is called a tyāgī. The practical definition of renunciation: not actionlessness but fruit-renunciation.

# 18.12
short: Desirable, undesirable, and mixed — these three kinds of fruits of action come to the non-relinquisher after death. But none at all for the sannyāsī.
long: "Aniṣṭam iṣṭaṃ miśraṃ ca tri-vidhaṃ karmaṇaḥ phalam / bhavaty atyāgināṃ pretya na tu sannyāsinām kvacit." Three kinds of fruits come "pretya" (after death) to those who have not renounced: "aniṣṭam" (undesirable), "iṣṭam" (desirable), "miśram" (mixed). "Bhavaty atyāgināṃ pretya" — comes to non-relinquishers after death. "Na tu sannyāsinām kvacit" — but for the sannyāsī (here meaning the true fruit-renouncer), none at all. The mechanism of karma: accumulated fruit-desire generates future existences. The fruit-renouncer breaks this chain.

# 18.14
short: The basis, the agent, the instruments of various kinds, the many distinct motions, and the divine as the fifth —
long: "Adhiṣṭhānaṃ tathā kartā karaṇaṃ ca pṛthag-vidham / vividhāś ca pṛthak ceṣṭā daivaṃ caivātra pañcamam." The five factors of action: (1) "Adhiṣṭhānam" — the basis/locus (the body as the field of action). (2) "Kartā" — the agent (the individual ego-sense, the one who thinks it acts). (3) "Karaṇaṃ ca pṛthag-vidham" — the instruments of various kinds (the senses, both cognitive and active, along with the mind). (4) "Vividhāś ca pṛthak ceṣṭāḥ" — the many distinct movements/functions (the physiological life-functions, prāṇa). (5) "Daivam... pañcamam" — the divine as the fifth (fate, prior karma, the cosmic factor).

# 18.15
short: Whatever action a person performs with body, speech, and mind — whether right or wrong — these five are its causes.
long: "Śarīra-vāṅ-manobhir yat karma prārabhate naraḥ / nyāyyaṃ vā viparītaṃ vā pañcaite tasya hetavaḥ." "Śarīra-vāṅ-manobhiḥ" — with body, speech, and mind. "Yat karma prārabhate naraḥ" — whatever action a person undertakes. "Nyāyyaṃ vā viparītaṃ vā" — whether righteous (nyāyya) or its opposite (viparīta — wrong, contrary to dharma). "Pañcaite tasya hetavaḥ" — these five (from 18.14) are its causes. The scope is total: every action, whether dharmic or adharmic, is produced by the interaction of these five factors. None is caused by the individual ego-agent alone.

# 18.16
short: This being so — one who sees the isolated self as the agent — that person, of distorted understanding, does not see clearly.
long: "Tatraivaṃ sati kartāram ātmānaṃ kevalaṃ tu yaḥ / paśyaty akṛta-buddhitvān na sa paśyati durmatiḥ." "Tatra evaṃ sati" — this being so (given the five-factor analysis). "Kartāram ātmānaṃ kevalam" — seeing the self (ātman) as the sole (kevala) agent. "Tu yaḥ paśyati" — the one who sees thus. "Akṛta-buddhitvāt" — due to incomplete/immature understanding (akṛta — unmade/incomplete). "Na sa paśyati durmatiḥ" — that person of distorted mind does not truly see. The philosophical point: claiming sole authorship of action ignores the five-factor causation. The sense of "I am the doer" is an over-simplification that creates bondage.

# 18.17
short: One whose nature is free from the ego-notion "I am the doer" — whose understanding is not stained — even if he slays these people, he does not slay and is not bound.
long: "Yasya nāhaṃkṛto bhāvo buddhir yasya na lipyate / hatvāpi sa imāl lokān na hanti na nibadhyate." "Yasya nāhaṃkṛto bhāvo" — one whose nature is free from the ahaṃkāra-driven sense of "I am the doer" (ahaṃ-kṛta — "I made/did"). "Buddhir yasya na lipyate" — whose understanding is not tainted/stained. "Hatvāpi sa imāl lokān" — even if he slays all these people. "Na hanti na nibadhyate" — he does not slay and is not bound. The radical statement: action performed without ahaṃkāra (the sense of personal doership) does not create karma-bondage. This is the philosophical underpinning of all of Krishna's earlier "fight, but don't identify with fighting."

# 18.22
short: But that which clings to one effect as if it were everything — irrational, without grasping the true — trivial — that is declared tamasic.
