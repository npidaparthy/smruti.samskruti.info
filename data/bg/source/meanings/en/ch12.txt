# 12.1
short: Arjuna asks: between those who worship You with form and those who worship the imperishable, unmanifest — who are the better knowers of yoga?
long: Arjuna poses the philosophical question that drives Chapter 12: which path is superior — saguna upāsanā (worship with form and attributes) or nirguṇa upāsanā (meditation on the formless Absolute)? This is not an idle question; it reflects the actual tension felt by seekers of all ages between devotion to a personal God and the more austere path of formless meditation. Kṛṣṇa's answer will be both inclusive and direct.

# 12.2
short: Kṛṣṇa answers: Those who worship Me with supreme faith, fixing their minds on Me — I consider them to be the most perfect yogīs.
long: Kṛṣṇa directly answers: the devotee who worships the personal form with full faith and mental absorption is the best yogī. This is the foundational statement of Bhakti Yoga. The word "mat-parāyaṇāḥ" (devoted to Me as the supreme) indicates complete surrender; "yuktā" indicates union. Faith combined with focused mind is Kṛṣṇa's definition of the highest yoga.

# 12.3
short: But those who worship the imperishable, indefinable, unmanifest, all-pervading, inconceivable, unchanging, unmovable, constant —
long: The verse begins the description of the nirguṇa path — meditation on the formless Absolute — without yet completing the judgment (that comes in 12.5). The attributes listed: imperishable (akṣaram), indefinable (anirdeśyam), unmanifest (avyaktam), all-pervading (sarvatra-gam), inconceivable (acintya), unchanging (kūṭastha), unmovable (acala), constant (dhruva). These are the qualities of Brahman without attributes.

# 12.4
short: — restraining all the senses, equal-minded in all conditions, devoted to the welfare of all beings — they also attain Me.
long: The practitioners of nirguṇa meditation also reach Kṛṣṇa — this is the inclusive answer. Three qualifications are essential: sense control (saṃniyamya indriya-grāmam), equanimity in all conditions (sarvatrasamabuddhayaḥ), and devotion to universal welfare (sarva-bhūta-hite ratāḥ). These are demanding requirements, and 12.5 explains why this path is harder than bhakti.

# 12.5
short: The difficulty is greater for those whose minds are set on the unmanifest, for the unmanifest path is hard to reach for the embodied.
long: Kṛṣṇa explains why saguna bhakti is easier: the embodied soul naturally relates to form, personality, and relationship. Meditating on the formless Absolute — which has no qualities, no name, no story, no face — requires a level of abstraction that is genuinely difficult for beings who live in bodies and relate through senses. This is not a criticism of the nirguṇa path, only an honest assessment of its difficulty for most practitioners.

# 12.6
short: But those who dedicate all their actions to Me, worship Me as supreme, meditate on Me, devoted to Me — I deliver them from the ocean of death and rebirth.
long: For those who practice total dedication (mat-karma), Me-as-supreme orientation (mat-paramāḥ), meditation on Me (mad-bhaktāḥ), and freedom from attachment — Kṛṣṇa personally becomes the deliverer. "Samudra" (ocean) — the saṃsāra of death and rebirth — is overcome not by the practitioner's effort alone but by divine grace responding to total surrender. This is the promise of Bhakti Yoga.

# 12.7
short: For those whose minds are fixed on Me, O Pārtha, I become swiftly the savior from the ocean of death and saṃsāra.
long: Kṛṣṇa emphasizes the swiftness of divine response when the devotee's mind is genuinely fixed on Him. "Na cirāt" — not long/soon — indicates that divine rescue is not delayed by cosmic bureaucracy. The relationship is immediate and personal: mind fixed on Me, and I respond. This directness is the distinctive promise of Bhakti Yoga over other paths that require longer discipline.

# 12.8
short: Fix your mind on Me alone, place your intellect in Me — henceforth you shall dwell in Me. No doubt about this.
long: The direct instruction begins: mind to Me (mayi eva manaḥ), intellect in Me (buddhiṃ niveśaya mayi). The result is immediate residence in the Divine — "mayi eva nivasiṣyasi." Kṛṣṇa adds "na saṃśayaḥ" — no doubt — with confidence. This is the most direct prescription in the Bhakti Yoga chapter: total mental and intellectual surrender to Kṛṣṇa as the practice.

# 12.9
short: If you cannot fix the mind steadily on Me, then by abhyāsa yoga — the yoga of practice — seek to reach Me, O Dhanañjaya.
long: Kṛṣṇa now builds a graduated path for those who cannot immediately achieve total absorption. If direct mental fixing is too difficult, then practice — abhyāsa — is the next step. Practice means repeatedly bringing the mind back to the Divine even when it wanders. The Gītā's teaching is compassionately layered: it meets the practitioner where they are and offers a path forward from that point.

# 12.10
short: If you are unable to do even this abhyāsa, then be devoted to performing action for My sake — performing actions for My sake, you shall attain perfection.
long: The second step down: if even practice is difficult, then karma-yoga dedicated to Kṛṣṇa is offered. "Mat-karma" — actions for Me, as offering, as service. The householder, the soldier, the teacher — any person in any station of life can practice this. Every action becomes sacred when performed as an offering to the Divine. This is the democratization of spiritual practice in the Gītā.

# 12.11
short: If even this is beyond you, then taking refuge in My yoga, renounce the fruits of all actions with a self-controlled mind.
long: The third step: if even dedicated action is too difficult, then at minimum — renounce the fruits of action. This is nishkama karma: action without attachment to results. "Ātma-vān" (self-controlled) is the qualification. Even this minimal practice — doing what you do but letting go of outcomes — is a valid path to Kṛṣṇa. The teaching accommodates every level of practitioner without dismissing any.

# 12.12
short: Better is knowledge than practice, better than knowledge is meditation, better than meditation is renunciation of fruit — from renunciation peace follows immediately.
long: Kṛṣṇa now ranks the practices in ascending order: mechanical practice < knowledge-based practice < meditation < renunciation of fruits. This does not contradict the earlier graduated path — it clarifies the principle behind it. Renunciation of fruits is highest because it directly removes the cause of bondage: the ego's claim on results. When results are surrendered, peace (śānti) follows immediately — "anantaram" — not eventually but at once.

# 12.13
short: The one who hates no being, who is friendly and compassionate to all, free from "mine" and ego, equal in pain and pleasure, patient —
long: Kṛṣṇa now describes the qualities of His beloved devotee — and they begin not with ritual or meditation but with the absence of hatred toward any being. The list: adveṣṭā (non-hating), maitrah (friendly), karuṇa (compassionate), nirmama (free from "mine"), nirāhaṃkāra (egoless), samaduḥkhasukha (equal in joy and sorrow), kṣamī (patient). These are not goals to achieve — they are the natural expression of bhakti matured into character.

# 12.15
short: The one from whom the world is not disturbed and who is not disturbed by the world, who is free from joy, envy, fear, and agitation — that one is dear to Me.
long: The spiritual person neither disturbs the world nor is disturbed by it. This "mutual non-disturbance" is a profound social ideal. Free from harṣa (elation), amarṣa (envy), bhaya (fear), udvega (agitation) — these four cover the main emotional reactivity patterns that make life turbulent. The fully settled devotee is a stabilizing presence wherever they are.

# 12.17
short: The one who neither rejoices nor hates, neither grieves nor desires, having renounced both auspicious and inauspicious — that devotee is dear to Me.
long: Equanimity in all four emotional directions: no joy at what is pleasant, no hate toward the unpleasant, no grief for what is lost, no desire for what is not yet attained. Beyond this, "śubhāśubha-parityāgī" — renouncing both the auspicious and the inauspicious. Even the auspicious (śubha) is transcended, because attachment to "good things" still binds. The devotee lives beyond preference, in a fullness that needs nothing added or removed.

# 12.18

# 12.20
short: Those who follow this immortal dharma as taught, full of faith, devoted to Me as the supreme goal — those devotees are exceedingly dear to Me.
long: The chapter closes with the supreme declaration: those who practice this "amṛta-dharma" (immortal dharma) with śraddhā (faith) and with Me as the supreme goal — these bhaktas are "atyartham priyāḥ" (exceedingly dear) to Kṛṣṇa. The word "atyartham" (beyond measure) is the Gītā's strongest expression of divine affection for the devotee. The entire chapter is a love letter from the Divine to the one who chooses devotion as the way home.
