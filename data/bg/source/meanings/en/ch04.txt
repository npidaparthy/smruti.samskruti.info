# 4.1
short: The Blessed Lord said: I taught this imperishable yoga to Vivasvān (the Sun-god); Vivasvān taught it to Manu; Manu instructed Ikṣvāku.
long: Chapter 4 opens with a startling claim: this yoga is ancient, transmitted from the cosmic beginning. Vivasvān (the Sun) received it from Kṛṣṇa, taught it to Manu (the progenitor of humanity), who taught it to Ikṣvāku (founder of the solar dynasty). The teaching has an unbroken lineage. Kṛṣṇa is not revealing something new — he is restoring what was lost.

# 4.2
short: This royal knowledge, inherited through succession, was known to the royal sages. But over time it was lost here on earth due to the length of time, O scorcher of enemies.
long: The teaching was preserved by the rājarṣis (royal sages — warrior-kings who were also knowers of Brahman, like Janaka). But the chain broke: "naṣṭaḥ parantapa" — it was lost. Time itself erodes transmission. Knowledge that is not actively practiced and taught deteriorates. This is why Kṛṣṇa must teach it again to Arjuna in this moment.

# 4.3
short: That same ancient yoga is today declared by Me to you; for you are My devotee and friend, and this is the supreme secret.
long: The teaching comes with a double qualification: Arjuna is Kṛṣṇa's bhakta (devotee) and sakhā (friend). The secret (rahasya) is not withheld for esoteric reasons — it requires the right relationship to receive. "Purātanam" — ancient. The yoga being taught in Kurukṣetra is the same truth that Vivasvān received at the cosmic dawn.

# 4.4
short: Arjuna said: Your birth was later; the birth of Vivasvān was earlier. How am I to understand that you taught this in the beginning?
long: A pointed chronological objection: Vivasvān predates Kṛṣṇa by cosmic eons. How could Kṛṣṇa have taught him? The question is not cynical but genuinely puzzled. Arjuna is asking about the nature of Kṛṣṇa's identity. The answer will be one of the Gītā's most profound statements — about divine incarnation and the eternal Self.

# 4.5
short: The Blessed Lord said: Many births of Mine have passed, and yours too, O Arjuna. I know all of them; you do not know them.
long: The divine claim: Kṛṣṇa has had many births — his incarnations are real. But unlike ordinary beings, he remembers them all: "veda sarvaḥ" — I know them all. Arjuna does not know his past births because he is caught in māyā (the power of delusion). The difference is not in the fact of birth but in the quality of awareness across births.

# 4.6
short: Though I am unborn, of imperishable nature, and am the Lord of all beings — yet, ruling over My own nature (prakṛti), I come into being by My own power (māyā).
long: The paradox of divine incarnation: unborn (ajo), imperishable (avyaya), Lord (Īśvara) — and yet: "sambhavāmy ātmamāyayā" — I come into being through My own power. This power (māyā) is not delusion but the Lord's creative self-manifestation. The incarnation is real but different in kind from human birth: it is chosen, not compelled. The Lord is never subject to karma or rebirth as ordinary souls are.

# 4.7
short: Whenever there is a decline of righteousness (dharma) and an uprising of unrighteousness, then I manifest Myself.
long: The principle of divine intervention: when dharma (the cosmic order, righteousness) declines and adharma (its opposite) rises, the Lord incarnates. "Tadātmānaṃ sṛjāmy aham" — I project Myself. This is not a reaction to failure but a cosmic rhythm: the Absolute participates in history through avatāras to preserve the conditions for human flourishing and spiritual growth.

# 4.8
short: For the protection of the good, for the destruction of the wicked, and for the establishment of dharma, I come into being age after age.
long: Three purposes of divine incarnation: protection of the sādhus (virtuous ones), destruction of the duṣkṛtas (evil-doers), and establishment of dharma. These three define the avatāra's mission across all incarnations — Rāma, Kṛṣṇa, the Boar, the Fish, all others. "Yuge yuge" — age after age — signals the recurring necessity of divine re-entry into history.

# 4.9
short: One who knows in truth My divine birth and actions — having left the body, does not come to birth again; such a person comes to Me, O Arjuna.
long: The soteriological claim: understanding Kṛṣṇa's birth as divine (divyam) and his actions as transcendent (karma) liberates the knower from rebirth. "Na punar janma" — no more birth. This is not a rewards system but a metaphysical claim: if one truly understands the nature of the divine, the identity between the devotee's Self and the Lord becomes clear, and the cycle of birth-death ends.

# 4.10
short: Freed from attachment, fear, and anger, absorbed in Me, taking refuge in Me, purified by the fire of knowledge — many have attained My nature.
long: Historical validation: it is not just a promise — many (bahavaḥ) have actually attained the divine state. The qualifications are: vīta-rāga-bhaya-krodha (freed from desire, fear, anger), man-mayā (absorbed in the Lord), mām upāśritāḥ (taking refuge). These purified beings attain "mad-bhāvam" — my state/nature. The path is proven.

# 4.11
short: In whatever way people take refuge in Me, I fulfill their desires in the same measure. O Arjuna, people follow My path in all ways.
long: The divine is universal in responsiveness: "ye yathā māṃ prapadyante" — however they approach, the divine meets them there. Those seeking material benefit receive material help; those seeking liberation receive liberation; those seeking knowledge receive knowledge. The Lord does not discriminate based on the form of approach. "Mama vartmā anuvartante" — all paths ultimately follow Mine — because all desire is ultimately desire for the Absolute.

# 4.12
short: Those who desire the fruits of action worship the gods here; for in this human world, success from action is quickly attained.
long: A neutral observation: action-oriented people worship specific deities (devas) for specific results — wealth, health, children, rain. And they get results quickly (kṣipraṃ hi). Kṛṣṇa does not condemn this — it is a valid level of practice. But the implication is clear: this path leads to conditioned, temporary results, not to the unconditioned. The Gītā's higher teaching points beyond deva-worship to the Lord himself.

# 4.13
short: The fourfold social order (varṇa) was created by Me according to the division of quality and action; though I am its creator, know Me to be non-acting and imperishable.
long: Kṛṣṇa claims authorship of the varṇa system (the four functional categories: brāhmaṇa, kṣatriya, vaiśya, śūdra) based on guṇa (quality of nature) and karma (function/action), not birth. This is the Gītā's understanding of social order — functional, not hereditary. And the crucial qualifier: the creator (sraṣṭā) remains non-acting (akartāram) and immutable (avyaya). The divine creates without being bound by the creation.

# 4.14
short: Actions do not taint Me, nor do I have any longing for the fruits of actions. One who understands Me thus is not bound by actions.
long: The reason for the Lord's non-bondage: na karmaphalasaṃspṛhaḥ — no longing for results. The divine acts without attachment to fruit, and therefore action does not bind. The one who truly understands this — who grasps the Lord's transcendence — models that same non-attachment and is also freed from bondage. Understanding the divine liberates, not merely theoretically but practically.

# 4.15
short: Having known this, the ancient seekers of liberation also performed actions; therefore you too should perform actions as the ancients did in ancient times.
long: Another appeal to tradition: the pūrva (ancient) mumukṣus (seekers of liberation) performed karmāṇi (actions) even while knowing the truth. They did not become inactive upon realization. Their example shows that knowledge and action are compatible — in fact, knowledge transforms the quality of action rather than ending it. "Tasmāt" — therefore — follows with logical necessity.

# 4.16

long: The problem with "action" and "inaction" is more subtle than it appears. Even the kavayaḥ (the poets/the wise) are confused. Kṛṣṇa will resolve this: the definition of action goes beyond physical movement. Understanding the true nature of action (karma), inaction (akarma), and forbidden action (vikarma) is the key to freedom. The Gītā's analysis of karma is one of its most philosophically rich sections.

# 4.17
short: One must understand action, as well as forbidden action, and also inaction — the nature of action is deep and difficult to penetrate.
long: A triple distinction: karma (action), vikarma (forbidden/wrong action), and akarma (inaction/non-action). All three require understanding. "Gahanā karmaṇo gatiḥ" — the nature of action is deep and hard to fathom. This is not a simple matter of "do this, don't do that." The philosophical depth of karma — its nature, its effects, when it binds and when it does not — is a profound topic.

# 4.18
short: One who sees inaction in action, and action in inaction — that person is wise among people; that person is a yogi who has accomplished all actions.
long: The most paradoxical statement in chapter 4: seeing akarma (non-action) in karma (action) and karma in akarma. What does this mean? The wise person who acts without ego-ownership (the Self is not the doer — 3.27) sees akarma in every action: the action happens through them, not by them. And they may "sit still" outwardly but the guṇas continue their play — so even in apparent inaction, there is always action. True inaction is at the level of the Self.

# 4.19
short: One whose undertakings are all free from desire and purpose, whose karma is burned by the fire of knowledge — that person is called a paṇḍita (wise one) by the wise.

Their karma is "jñānāgni-dagdha" — burned by the fire of knowledge. This burning is not destruction but transformation: action performed in knowledge no longer accumulates as binding karma. # 4.20

This is not physical inaction but the inner reality of the liberated person: the Self does not do; the body-mind-guṇas do. The person acts, but the true Self remains uninvolved. "Tyaktvā karma-phalāsaṅgam" — having abandoned attachment to results — is the key condition.

# 4.24
short: Brahman is the offering; Brahman is the oblation poured by Brahman into the fire of Brahman. Brahman alone is to be reached by one who is absorbed in the action of Brahman.
long: The most radical formulation of yajña: every element of the sacrifice is Brahman. The ladle (offering instrument), the ghee (oblation), the fire, the act of pouring — all are Brahman. The doer absorbed in this vision sees only Brahman everywhere. Such a sacrifice does not accumulate karma because there is no separate doer. This verse has been used as a blessing (āhuti-mantra) before meals in Vedāntic traditions.

# 4.26
short: Some offer hearing and the other senses into the fires of restraint; others offer sound and other objects of the senses into the fires of the senses.
long: Two more forms of internal yajña: hearing (śrotrādīni — senses) poured into the fire of saṃyama (restraint). And inversely: sound and other sense objects poured into the fires of the senses themselves — meaning, the senses become the fire that "burns up" their objects by offering them as yajña. Both approaches transform ordinary sensory activity into spiritual practice.
